# CJEU C-131/12: Google Spain (Right to Be Forgotten)

## Case Information
- **Court:** Court of Justice of the European Union
- **Citation:** ECLI:EU:C:2014:317
- **Date:** May 13, 2014

## Facts

Mario Costeja Gonzalez complained to the Spanish DPA (AEPD) in 2010 after finding links to 1998 newspaper articles mentioning his name in Google search results. These articles referenced a real estate auction related to his social security debt recovery proceedings. While the newspaper's publication was lawful, the AEPD upheld his complaint against Google, ordering removal of the search results.

## Key Holdings

**Data Processing & Control:** Search engine activities -- finding, indexing, storing, and presenting internet information -- constitute "personal data processing." Google qualifies as a "controller" because it "determines the purposes and means of that activity."

**Establishment & Jurisdiction:** Spanish jurisdiction applied where Google maintained advertising sales operations targeting Spanish residents.

**Removal Obligations:** Search engine operators must remove links from name-based search results when information is "inadequate, irrelevant or no longer relevant" relative to original collection purposes -- even when third-party publication remains lawful.

**Right to Be Forgotten:** Data subjects possess rights to request removal of outdated personal information from search results, balancing privacy against public interest.

## Significance

This landmark ruling established that search engines bear direct responsibility for personal data they index, fundamentally shaping GDPR-era data protection enforcement and establishing the "right to be forgotten" as a core principle in EU law.

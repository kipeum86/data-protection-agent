# CJEU C-362/14: Schrems I

## Case Information
- **Court:** Court of Justice of the European Union
- **Citation:** ECLI:EU:C:2015:650
- **Date:** October 6, 2015
- **Parties:** Maximilian Schrems v. Data Protection Commissioner (Ireland)

## Facts

Austrian citizen Maximilian Schrems used Facebook since 2008. Facebook Ireland transferred his personal data to parent company Facebook Inc. servers in the United States. The concern centered on NSA and US security agency access to this data through mass surveillance programs. EU citizens lacked effective remedies regarding such surveillance activities.

The European Commission had previously deemed US data transfers lawful under the Safe Harbor regime (Decision 2000/520), which included derogations permitting US national security access to transferred data.

## Key Holdings

### Primary Ruling
An Adequacy Decision does not prevent DPAs from examining data subject complaints regarding inadequate third-country protection. Complainants retain access to judicial remedies per Article 47 CFR.

### Protection Standard
"Adequate" protection requires "essentially equivalent" levels to EU standards. The Commission must assess whether third countries' domestic law and compliance practices provide such equivalence.

### Invalidation of Safe Harbor
1. **Blanket access provisions violate fundamental rights:** Legislation authorizing "generalised" storage and access to all transferred EU personal data without differentiation or limitation compromises the essence of privacy protection under Article 7 CFR.
2. **Insufficient remedies:** Legislation preventing individuals from accessing personal data about themselves or obtaining rectification/erasure violates the essence of effective judicial protection under Article 47 CFR.
3. **Excessive Commission authority:** Article 3 of the Adequacy Decision improperly restricted DPA powers.

## Significance

This landmark decision invalidated the primary legal mechanism enabling US-EU data transfers for over 15 years (Safe Harbor). It established that fundamental rights limitations must apply "only in so far as is strictly necessary" and that mass surveillance frameworks cannot satisfy adequacy standards.

# CJEU C-311/18: Facebook Ireland and Schrems (Schrems II)

## Case Information
- **Court:** Court of Justice of the European Union
- **Citation:** ECLI:EU:C:2020:559
- **Decision Date:** July 16, 2020
- **Parties:** Data Protection Commission, Facebook Ireland, Maximilian Schrems

## Facts

Austrian citizen Maximilian Schrems used Facebook since 2008. Facebook Ireland transferred his data to Facebook Inc.'s US servers. In 2013, Schrems complained to Ireland's Data Protection Commissioner seeking to halt these transfers. After rejection, he appealed to the Irish High Court, which referred questions to the CJEU.

The initial "Schrems I" judgment (2015) invalidated the Safe Harbor adequacy decision. On remittal, Facebook claimed Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) governed the transfers. Schrems alleged US surveillance laws -- specifically FISA Section 702 and Executive Order 12.333 -- violated data protection principles and Charter Articles 7, 8, and 47.

The High Court found these programs enabled mass surveillance of non-US citizens abroad without equivalent protections afforded to US citizens.

## Key Holdings

1. **GDPR Scope:** GDPR applies to commercial data transfers even when national authorities subsequently process data for security purposes.

2. **Protection Standard:** Transferred data requires "essentially equivalent" protection to EU standards, assessed by examining both contractual safeguards and actual state authority access patterns.

3. **Privacy Shield Invalidated:** Decision 2016/1250 violated Charter provisions. US surveillance requirements were not proportionate, lacked "strict necessity" limits, and provided no enforceable judicial remedies for non-citizens.

4. **Ombudsperson Inadequate:** The mechanism lacked full independence and could not bind intelligence services.

5. **SCCs Remain Valid:** Standard Contractual Clauses themselves are valid despite not binding third-country governments. However, validity depends on effective compliance mechanisms existing in practice.

6. **Transfer Suspension Duty:** Supervisory authorities must suspend or prohibit transfers where SCCs cannot be complied with in the third country.

## Significance

This landmark decision invalidated the primary legal basis for EU-US data transfers (Privacy Shield) while preserving SCCs as viable transfer mechanisms -- provided controllers conduct adequate impact assessments and suspend transfers where real-world protection proves inadequate.

                                  19   II.    LEGAL STANDARD

                                  20          A motion to dismiss under Rule 12(b)(6) tests the legal sufficiency of the claims alleged in

                                  21   the complaint. See Parks Sch. of Bus., Inc. v. Symington, 51 F.3d 1480, 1484 (9th Cir. 1995). When

                                  22   reviewing a motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim, the court must “accept as true all of the

                                  23   factual allegations contained in the complaint,” Erickson, 551 U.S. at 94, and may dismiss a claim

                                  24   “only where there is no cognizable legal theory” or there is an absence of “sufficient factual matter

                                  25   to state a facially plausible claim to relief,” Shroyer v. New Cingular Wireless Servs., Inc., 622 F.3d

                                  26   1035, 1041 (9th Cir. 2010) (citing Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662, 677-78 (2009); Navarro v. Block,

                                  27   250 F.3d 729, 732 (9th Cir. 2001)) (internal quotation marks omitted). A claim has facial

                                  28   plausibility when a plaintiff “pleads factual content that allows the court to draw the reasonable
                                                                                          3
                                       Case 4:25-cv-02124-DMR             Document 36         Filed 09/02/25     Page 4 of 11




                                   1   inference that the defendant is liable for the misconduct alleged.” Iqbal, 556 U.S. at 678 (citation

                                   2   omitted). In other words, the facts alleged must demonstrate “more than labels and conclusions,

                                   3   and a formulaic recitation of the elements of a cause of action will not do.” Bell Atl. Corp. v.

                                   4   Twombly, 550 U.S. 554, 555 (2007).

                                   5           Under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 15(a), leave to amend should be granted as a matter

                                   6   of course, at least until the defendant files a responsive pleading. Fed. R. Civ. P. 15(a)(1). After that

                                   7   point, Rule 15(a) provides generally that leave to amend the pleadings before trial should be given

                                   8   “freely . . . when justice so requires.” Fed. R. Civ. P. 15(a)(2). “This policy is to be applied with

                                   9   extreme liberality.” Eminence Capital, LLC v. Aspeon, Inc., 316 F.3d 1048, 1051 (9th Cir. 2003)

                                  10   (quotation omitted). However, leave to amend may be denied where the complaint “could not be

                                  11   saved by any amendment,” i.e., “where the amendment would be futile.” Thinket Ink Info. Res., Inc.

                                  12   v. Sun Microsystems, Inc., 368 F.3d 1053, 1061 (9th Cir. 2004).

                                   8           C.      Allegations about Trackers as Pen Registers

                                   9           Keleops argues the CIPA claim fails because Garon has not alleged the existence of a pen

                                  10   register for two reasons. First, Keleops contends that the FAC “alleges the transmission of

                                  11   Plaintiff’s IP address,” but that “[r]ecording an IP address is not equivalent to recording the numbers

                                  12   dialed from a phone and does not make any record of other outgoing routing, dialing, or signaling


 United States District Court
                                  13   information.” Mot. at 9. Second, Keleops asserts that software is not a “device or process” within

                                  14   the meaning of CIPA. Id. at 10-14.

                                  15           CIPA provides that “a person may not install or use a pen register . . . without first obtaining



Northern District of California
                                  16   a court order[.]” Cal. Penal Code § 638.51. A “pen register” is “a device or process that records or

                                  17   decodes dialing, routing, addressing, or signaling information transmitted by an instrument or

                                  18   facility from which a wire or electronic communication is transmitted, but not the contents of a

                                  19   communication.” Cal. Penal Code § 638.50(b).

                                  20           The FAC contains sufficient allegations that the Trackers constitute “pen registers” within

                                  21   the meaning of CIPA. First, the FAC adequately alleges that the Trackers constitute a “device or

                                  22   process.” See, e.g., FAC ¶ 91 (“The Steamrail Tracker is at least a ‘process’ because it is ‘software

                                  23   that identifies consumers, gathers data, and correlates that data.’”) (citation omitted); id. ¶ 92 (“[T]he

                                  24   Steamrail Tracker is a ‘device’ because ‘in order for software to work, it must be run on some kind

                                  25   of computing device.’”) (citation omitted); id. ¶ 101 (“The AGKN Tracker is at least a ‘process’

                                  26   because it is ‘software that identifies consumers, gathers data, and correlates that data.’”) (citation

                                  27   omitted); id. ¶ 102 (“[T]he AGKN Tracker is a ‘device’ because ‘in order for software to work, it

                                  28   must be run on some kind of computing device.’”) (citation omitted); id. ¶ 112 (“The Crowd Control
                                                                                           5

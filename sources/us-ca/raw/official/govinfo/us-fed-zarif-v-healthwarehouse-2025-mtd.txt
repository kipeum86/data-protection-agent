18                                 PERSONAL JURISDICTION
19   I.    Legal Standard
20         When raised as a defense by motion, Rule 12(b)(2) authorizes the dismissal of an
21   action for lack of personal jurisdiction. Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(2). In this circumstance, “the
22   plaintiff bears the burden of demonstrating that jurisdiction is appropriate.” Will Co. v.
23   Lee, 47 F.4th 917, 921 (9th Cir. 2022). When the defendant’s motion is based on written
24   materials, and no evidentiary hearing is held, the court evaluates only whether the plaintiff
25   demonstrates a prima facie showing of personal jurisdiction based on the plaintiff’s
26   pleadings and affidavits. Id. The court must take unchallenged allegations in the complaint
27   as true, and conflicts between the parties over statements within any affidavits must be
28   resolved in favor of the plaintiff. Id.

 1   II.   Analysis
 2         Health Warehouse renews its argument that the Court lacks personal jurisdiction.
 3   (Mot. 6:13–8:21.) Jurisdiction over a defendant is proper if it is permitted by a long-arm
 4   statute and if the exercise of that jurisdiction does not violate federal due process. Pebble
 5   Beach Co. v. Caddy, 453 F.3d 1151, 1154 (9th Cir. 2006). For due process to be satisfied,
 6   a defendant must have “minimum contacts” within the forum state such that asserting
 7   jurisdiction over the defendant would not “offend traditional notions of fair play and
 8   substantial justice.” Id. at 1155 (citing Int’l Shoe Co. v. Washington, 326 U.S. 310, 315
 9   (1945)). Both the California and federal long-arm statutes require compliance with due
10   process requirements. Daimler AG v. Bauman, 571 U.S. 117, 125 (2014).
11         There are two types of personal jurisdiction: general and specific. Id. at 118. Only
12   the second one is relevant here.      Specific jurisdiction permits the court to exercise
13   jurisdiction over a defendant who has availed itself through forum-related activities that
14   gave rise to the action before the court. Bancroft & Masters, Inc. v. August Nat’l Inc., 223
15   F.3d 1082, 1086 (9th Cir. 2000), overruled on other grounds in part by Yahoo! Inc. v. La
16   Ligue Contre Le Racisme Et L’Antisemitisme, 433 F.3d 1199 (9th Cir. 2006). The Ninth
17   Circuit uses a three-pronged test for specific jurisdiction:
18         (1) The non-resident defendant must purposefully direct [its] activities or
               consummate some transaction with the forum or resident thereof; or
19
               perform some act by which [it] purposefully avails [itself] of the privilege
20             of conducting activities in the forum, thereby invoking the benefits and
               protections of its laws;
21
22         (2) the claim must be one which arises out of or relates to the defendant’s
               forum-related activities; and
23
24         (3) the exercise of jurisdiction must comport with fair play and substantial
               justice, i.e. it must be reasonable.
25
26   Schwarzenegger v. Fred Martin Motor Co., 374 F.3d 797, 802 (9th Cir. 2004). The
27   plaintiff bears the burden of proving the first two prongs and, if successful, the burden
28   shifts to the defendant on the third prong to prove that jurisdiction is unreasonable. Id.

 1         A.    Purposeful Direction
 2         As to prong one, for claims sounding in tort like those brought here, courts look to
 3   whether a defendant purposefully directed its activities at the forum state. Yahoo!, 433
 4   F.3d at 1206. Courts apply the “effects” test from Calder v. Jones, 465 U.S. 783 (1984),
 5   to make this determination.
 6         The Calder effects test asks “whether the defendant: ‘(1) committed an intentional
 7   act, (2) expressly aimed at the forum state, (3) causing harm that the defendant knows is
 8   likely to be suffered in the forum state.’”        Will Co., 47 F.4th at 922 (quoting
 9   Schwarzenegger, 374 F.3d at 803); see also Ford Motor Co. v. Mont. Eighth Jud. Dist. Ct.,
10   592 U.S. 351, 361 (2021). All three components must be met for a defendant to have
11   purposefully directed its activities to the forum state. Pakootas v. Teck Cominco Metals,
12   Ltd., 905 F.3d 565, 577 (9th Cir. 2018).
13               1.     Intentional Act
14         The first component of the Calder effects test requires an intentional act by the
15   defendant. Ayla, LLC v. Alya Skin Pty. Ltd., 11 F.4th 972, 980 (9th Cir. 2021). This
16   requirement is met when the defendant acts with an “intent to perform an actual, physical
17   act in the real world, rather than an intent to accomplish a result or consequence of that
18   act.” AMA Multimedia, LLC v. Wanat, 970 F.3d 1201, 1209 (9th Cir. 2020) (quoting
19   Schwarzenegger, 374 F.3d at 806). Operating a website is an intentional act. Id. A
20   defendant’s “sale of [physical] products to [the forum’s] residents” is likewise “an
21   intentional act.” Herbal Brands, Inc. v. Photoplaza, Inc., 72 F.4th 1085, 1091 (9th Cir.
22   2023).
23         Plaintiff alleges Health Warehouse operates its “website as a storefront to make
24   product sales,” and the pharmacy delivers physical products to California residents. (TAC
25   ¶¶ 28, 36.) Accordingly, the intentional act prong is satisfied. See AMA, 970 F.3d at 1209;
26   Herbal Brands, 72 F.4th at 1091.

 1                2.     Express Aiming
 2         For the second element of the Calder effects test, the plaintiff must sufficiently
 3   allege a defendant “expressly aimed” its intentional act at the forum. Ayla, 11 F.4th at 980.
 4   The Court addressed this requirement in its two prior orders.
 5                       i.    First Dismissal Order
 6         The First Amended Complaint included allegations about Health Warehouse’s
 7   website, but the pleading lacked the more detailed allegations about Health Warehouse’s
 8   business summarized above, including that the pharmacy has delivered “physical products
 9   to thousands of California residents” and promoted its online store to California residents.
10   (Compare First Amend. Compl. ¶¶ 38–43, ECF No. 9, with TAC ¶¶ 36–66.) Hence, when
11   Health Warehouse moved to dismiss under Rule 12(b)(2), the Court predominantly looked
12   to the Ninth Circuit’s website cases to assess the express aiming requirement. (First
13   Dismissal Order 5:17–6:11, ECF No. 17.)
14         Under those cases, there must be “something more to indicate that the defendant
15   purposefully (albeit electronically) directed [its] activity in a substantial way to the forum
16   state.” Cybersell Inc. v. Cybersell Inc., 130 F.3d 418 (9th Cir. 1997). “Not all material
17   placed on the Internet is, solely by virtue of its universal accessibility, expressly aimed at
18   every state in which it is accessed.” Mavrix Photo, Inc. v. Brand Techs., Inc., 647 F.3d
19   1218, 1231 (9th Cir. 2011). But where “a website with national viewership and scope
20   appeals to, and profits from, an audience in a particular state, the site’s operators can be
21   said to have ‘expressly aimed’ at that state.” Id.
22         Applying that line of authority, this Court found Plaintiff did not allege the
23   “something more” required for express aiming. (First Dismissal Order 7:3–22.) She did
24   “not allege that any products were shipped to California.” (Id. 7:17–18.) And although
25   Plaintiff claimed Health Warehouse marketed to Californians, she did not provide any
26   specifics. (Id. 7:13–15.) Thus, the Court dismissed the First Amended Complaint for lack
27   of personal jurisdiction but gave Plaintiff leave to amend. (Id. 8:2–4.)

17                       iii.   Express Aiming Absent Briskin
18         As the Court turns to Plaintiff’s Third Amended Complaint, the ground has shifted
19   in two spots. First, Plaintiff added examples of Health Warehouse’s attempts to woo
20   California customers to her pleading. (TAC ¶ 41.) Second, the Ninth Circuit vacated its
21   opinion in Briskin and granted a rehearing en banc. 101 F.4th 706 (9th Cir. 2024).
22         The question, then, is whether Plaintiff demonstrates express aiming under the Ninth
23   Circuit’s caselaw absent Briskin. The Court finds that she does. First, as mentioned,
24   Plaintiff includes allegations showing Health Warehouse attempted to appeal to and profit
25   from California customers in particular. See Mavrix Photo, 647 F.3d at 1231.
26         Health Warehouse argues its promotional efforts from the early 2010s “are entirely
27   irrelevant to any pertinent analysis.” (Mot. 7:21–22.) The Court is not persuaded. Plaintiff
28   alleges these marketing efforts helped Health Warehouse target California customers,

 1   cultivate a California market, and deliver “physical products to thousands of California
 2   residents in California over the past decade or more.” (TAC ¶¶ 28, 42–48.) On the limited
 3   record before the Court, the implication is clear that Health Warehouse found success in
 4   targeting and exploiting the California market with its website. See Will Co., 47 F.4th at
 5   921 (providing “[u]ncontroverted allegations in the complaint must be taken as true”).
 6         Second, even setting aside the targeting of California residents, now that Briskin has
 7   been vacated, the case of Herbal Brands, Inc. v. Photoplaza, Inc., 72 F.4th 1085 (9th Cir.
 8   2023), controls. Following Herbal Brands, this Court finds Health Warehouse expressly
 9   aimed its conduct at the forum by delivering physical products to thousands of California
10   residents. “Although the internet can be dizzyingly complex, for jurisdictional purposes,
11   the act of selling physical products over the internet to a forum resident is substantially the
12   same as selling those same products to a forum resident through a mail-order catalog.” 72
13   F.4th at 1095. In other words, the sale of products to forum residents would provide the
14   requisite “something more” even if Health Warehouse had not marketed its website to
15   California residents. Id. at 1093. Thus, the Third Amended Complaint’s factual allegations
16   are sufficient to suggest Health Warehouse expressly aimed its conduct at California.
17                3.     Harm in the Forum
18         The third prong of the Calder effects test asks whether the defendant caused harm
19   that it knew is likely to be suffered in the forum state. Will Co., 47 F.4th at 922. “[T]his
20   element does not require that the ‘brunt’ of the harm be suffered in the forum, . . . and . . .
21   may be established even if ‘the bulk of the harm’ occurs outside the forum.” Brayton
22   Purcell LLP v. Recordon & Recordon, 606 F.3d 1124, 1131 (9th Cir. 2010) (citing Yahoo!,
23   433 F.3d at 1207), abrogated on other grounds as recognized by Axiom Foods, Inc. v.
24   Acerchem Int’l, Inc., 874 F.3d 1064, 1069 (9th Cir. 2017).
25         Plaintiff alleges Health Warehouse “knew that it was going to harm California
26   residents by enabling Facebook and other spyware companies to wiretap the residents’
27   communications.” (TAC ¶ 53.) The company knew “specifically which users were in
28   California,” and Defendant purportedly knew it was improperly using tracking tools to

 1   capture users’ information. (Id. ¶¶ 53, 57, 155.) These allegations are sufficient to show

 3   Will Co., 47 F.4th at 922; see also Swarts v. Home Depot, Inc., 689 F. Supp. 3d 732, 741

 8   Health Warehouse purposefully directed its conduct towards California.
 9         B.     Relating to Forum Activities
10         “The second prong of the specific jurisdiction inquiry requires that a plaintiff’s
11   claims ‘arise out of or relate to the defendant’s contacts’ with the forum.” Herbal Brands,
12   72 F.4th at 1096 (quoting Ford Motor, 592 U.S. at 359). “The first half of that standard
13   asks about causation; but the back half, after the ‘or,’ contemplates that some relationships
14   will support jurisdiction without a causal showing.” Ford Motor, 592 U.S. at 362.
15         Health Warehouse argues “none of the forum-related activities alleged are even
16   tangentially related to Plaintiff’s claims.” (Mot. 7:2–6.) This point is unavailing. Plaintiff
17   alleges she is a customer of Healthwarehouse.com and browsed the site “to determine
18   availability and pricing for prescription drugs.” (TAC ¶ 121.) A California customer
19   browsing and shopping for prescription drugs is plainly related to Health Warehouse’s
20   forum activities of marketing, selling, and shipping prescription drugs to California
21   residents. Further, Health Warehouse monitored and captured Plaintiff’s browsing activity
22   “to increase [its] business in the forum and to collect and share valuable data from
23   consumers in California.” (Id. ¶ 52; see also id. ¶¶ 55, 135.) Thus, Plaintiff sufficiently
24   alleges that her claims arise out of or relate to Health Warehouse’s contacts with the forum.
25   See Herbal Brands, 72 F.4th at 1096; see also Swarts, 689 F. Supp. 3d at 743 (reasoning
26   data collection claims arising from website chat feature related to the defendant’s sales and
27   business contacts in California); Rodriguez v. Aquatic Sales Sols. LLC, No. 2:23-CV-
28   05198-CAS-EX, 2024 WL 2804097, at *5 (C.D. Cal. May 29, 2024) (same).

 1         C.     Reasonableness
 2         Because Plaintiff satisfies the first two prongs of the specific jurisdiction test, the
 3   burden shifts to Health Warehouse “to ‘present a compelling case’ that the exercise of
 4   jurisdiction would not be reasonable.” Schwarzenegger, 374 F.3d at 802 (quoting Burger
 5   King Corp. v. Rudzewicz, 471 U.S. 462, 476–78 (1985)). Health Warehouse does not
 6   attempt to make this showing here. (See Mot. 6:13–8:7.) Hence, the three-part specific
 7   jurisdiction test is satisfied. See Schwarzenegger, 374 F.3d at 802.
 8         In sum, the Court finds exercising specific jurisdiction over Health Warehouse is
 9   appropriate, and the Court denies the Motion to Dismiss under Rule 12(b)(2).
10                               FAILURE TO STATE A CLAIM
11   I.    Legal Standard
12         To avoid a Rule 12(b)(6) dismissal, a complaint need not contain detailed factual
13   allegations; rather, it must plead “enough facts to state a claim to relief that is plausible on
14   its face.” Bell Atl. Corp. v. Twombly, 550 U.S. 544, 570 (2007). “A claim has facial
15   plausibility when the plaintiff pleads factual content that allows the court to draw the
16   reasonable inference that the defendant is liable for the misconduct alleged.” Ashcroft v.
17   Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662, 678 (2009) (citing Twombly, 550 U.S. at 556). “Where a complaint
18   pleads facts that are ‘merely consistent with’ a defendant’s liability, it ‘stops short of the
19   line between possibility and plausibility of entitlement to relief.’” Id. (quoting Twombly,
20   550 U.S. at 557).
21   II.   Analysis
22         A.     Plaintiff’s Consent
23         Health Warehouse’s first argument is a global one. It contends Plaintiff consented
24   to her data being collected, which would bar her privacy claims. (Mot. 9:1–11:16.)
25         However, Plaintiff repeatedly alleges she did not consent to Health Warehouse’s
26   conduct. (E.g., TAC ¶¶ 9, 70–71, 121, 129.) So, regardless of which party holds the burden
27   of proof on this point, the factual allegations show Plaintiff’s consent was lacking. See,
28   e.g., India Price v. Carnival Corp., 712 F. Supp. 3d 1347, 1357 (S.D. Cal. 2024) (Curiel,

     Case 3:23-cv-00565-BAS-DEB         Document 32 Filed 02/13/25        PageID.<pageID>
                                         Page 11 of 26


 1   J.) (reasoning a defendant’s “consent defense appears premature” at the motion to dismiss
 2   phase “where the Court must take as true the Plaintiffs’ well-pleaded factual allegations”).
 3         Recognizing this challenge, Health Warehouse asks the Court to consider several
 4   policies from Facebook’s owner—Meta Platforms, Inc. Health Warehouse argues these
 5   policies show Plaintiff “consented to share information with Facebook when [she]
 6   registered as [a] Facebook user[] and [was] informed that Facebook tracks off-Facebook
 7   web activities, including those captured on third-party websites via Meta’s Pixel.” (Mot.
 8   9:15–22.) This argument does not go far. First, although these policies may address
 9   tracking through Meta’s Pixel tool, the policies do not address Plaintiff’s allegation that
10   Health Warehouse “deployed numerous other spyware software devices on its website that,
11   much like [Meta’s] Pixel, contemporaneously and surreptitiously intercept[ed]” Plaintiff’s
12   activity for “data mining” to do advertising and marketing. (TAC ¶ 155; see also id.
13   ¶¶ 156–88.)
14         Second, for these policies to defeat Plaintiff’s claims at this stage, the policies must
15   have only one plausible interpretation showing Plaintiff consented to the conduct. See In
16   re Meta Pixel Healthcare Litig., 647 F. Supp. 3d 778, 794 (N.D. Cal. 2022). “For consent
17   to be actual, the disclosures must ‘explicitly notify’ users of the conduct at issue.” Calhoun
18   v. Google, LLC, 113 F.4th 1141, 1147 (9th Cir. 2024). “In other words, consent is only
19   effective if the person alleging harm consented ‘to the particular conduct, or to substantially
20   the same conduct’ and if the alleged tortfeasor did not exceed the scope of that consent.”
21   Brown v. Google LLC, 685 F. Supp. 3d 909, 926 (N.D. Cal. 2023) (citing Restatement
22   (Second) of Torts § 892A (1979)).          Here, Defendant does not show the policies
23   unambiguously allowed it to capture and share information like the specific prescription
24   drugs Plaintiff was searching for to purchase for herself and family members. Cf. Meta
25   Pixel Healthcare Litig., 647 F. Supp. 3d at 793 (reasoning the “generalized notice” in
26   Meta’s policies did not “specifically indicate that Meta may acquire health data”); Castillo
27   v. Costco Wholesale Corp., No. 2:23-CV-01548-JHC, 2024 WL 4785136, at *12 (W.D.
28   Wash. Nov. 14, 2024) (rejecting a similar argument involving online pharmacy services).

 3         B.     Wiretap Act
 4                1.    Contents of Any Communication
 5         Health Warehouse raises several challenges to Plaintiff’s first claim for violation of
 6   the Wiretap Act. Initially, Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot state a claim because Health
 7   Warehouse did not intercept the “contents” of any communication. (Mot. 11:17–12:20.)
 8   Health Warehouse contends Plaintiff’s “mouse clicks and movements, keystrokes, search
 9   terms, and pages and content viewed by [her]” are not “contents” under the Wiretap Act.
10   (Mot. 11:8–10 (quoting Jacome v. Spirit Airlines Inc., No. 2021-000947-CA-01, 2021 WL
11   3087860, at *4 (Fla. Cir. Ct. June 17, 2021)).)
12         With exceptions, the Wiretap Act—also known as the Electronic Communications
13   Privacy Act—prohibits a person from intentionally using or disclosing to any other person
14   the “contents” of an intercepted electronic communication. 18 U.S.C. § 2511(1)(c)–(d). A
15   communication’s “contents” encompass “any information concerning the substance,
16   purport, or meaning of that communication.” Id. § 2510(8).
17         In Zynga Privacy Litigation, the Ninth Circuit interpreted the definition of
18   “contents” to mean “the intended message conveyed by the communication.” 750 F.3d
19   1098, 1106 (9th Cir. 2014). The contents of a communication are distinguishable from the
20   “record information,” which is “information regarding the characteristics of the message
21   that is generated in the course of the communication.” Id. For example, in the phone call
22   setting, the call’s “origination, length, and time” are not the call’s contents—but instead
23   record information—because those items contain “no ‘information concerning the
24   substance, purport, or meaning of [the] communication.’” United States v. Reed, 575 F.3d
25   900, 917 (9th Cir. 2009) (alteration in original) (quoting 18 U.S.C. § 2510(8)).
26         Zynga Privacy Litigation concerned web browser requests that included a “referrer
27   header that provided both the user’s Facebook ID and the address of the Facebook webpage
28   the user was viewing when the user clicked the link.” 750 F.3d at 1102. The Ninth Circuit

                                                - 12 -
                                                                                         23cv0565
     Case 3:23-cv-00565-BAS-DEB       Document 32 Filed 02/13/25       PageID.<pageID>
                                       Page 13 of 26


 1   reasoned this information was record information because it included “only basic
 2   identification and address information, not a search term or similar communication made
 3   by the user.” Id. at 1109.
 4         The Ninth Circuit anticipated that in other circumstances, however, a user’s request
 5   to a search engine for specific information could constitute a communication where a web
 6   address included the user’s search term. Id. 1108–09. Further, the Ninth Circuit approved
 7   a First Circuit decision that concluded a “defendant was disclosing the contents of a
 8   communication” where “the users had communicated with the website by entering their
 9   personal medical information into a form provided by a website.” Id. at 1107 (citing In re
10   Pharmatrak, Inc., 329 F.3d 9, 18–19 (1st Cir. 2003)).
11         Plaintiff’s allegations encapsulate more than unprotected “record information.” She
12   claims Health Warehouse disclosed her “searches for prescription medications for herself,
13   her infant, and her elderly father.” (TAC ¶ 121; see also id. ¶¶ 124–26 (providing examples
14   of the searches).) The search terms Plaintiff entered into Health Warehouse’s website
15   concern “the substance, purport, or meaning of that communication.” See 18 U.S.C. §
16   2510(8); accord Meta Pixel Healthcare Litig., 647 F. Supp. 3d at 796. Thus, Plaintiff
17   plausibly alleges Health Warehouse intercepted the contents of a communication. See, e.g.,
18   Brown, 685 F. Supp. 3d at 935–36 (reasoning web addresses that reveal general private
19   browsing data constitute “contents” under the Wiretap Act); cf. In re Facebook, Inc.
20   Internet Tracking Litig., 956 F.3d 589, 605 (9th Cir. 2020) (discussing Zynga and reasoning
21   search “terms and the resulting URLs could divulge a user’s personal interests, queries,
22   and habits”). Thus, this challenge to the Wiretap Act claim is unpersuasive.
23                2.    Intentional Interception
24         Health Warehouse next argues Plaintiff “has not alleged the requisite intent” for her
25   Wiretap Act claim. (Mot. 12:21–13:13.) The statute imposes liability on a person who
26   “intentionally” intercepts or discloses protected communications. 18 U.S.C. § 2511(a).
27   Health Warehouse argues its conduct was not intentional because it “had no reason to
28

                                                                                         23cv0565

                                         Page 14 of 26


 1   know” that using “a widely deployed website software for ad optimization could violate
 2   wiretapping and hacking statutes.” (Mot. 13:10–12.)
 3         Caselaw forecloses this challenge.        The Ninth Circuit rejected that the word
 4   “intentionally” in the Wiretap Act “must be read to require a defendant to know that [its]
 5   conduct is unlawful.” United States v. Christensen, 828 F.3d 763, 774 (9th Cir. 2015).
 6   Rather, the statute only requires “an intentional act, defined as ‘an act that is being done on
 7   purpose.’” Id. at 775 (quoting S. Rep. No. 99–541, at 24 (1986)). Hence, “the operative
 8   question under [the Wiretap Act] is whether the defendant acted consciously and
 9   deliberately with the goal of intercepting wire communications.” Id.
10         Plaintiff alleges Health Warehouse acted deliberately when it deployed tracking
11   software that intercepted her communications. (TAC ¶¶ 8, 15, 67, 99.) Her allegations are
12   enough to allege Health Warehouse acted intentionally, and Defendant’s argument to the
13   contrary is unavailing.
14                3.     Interception While in Transit
15         Health Warehouse seeks dismissal of the Wiretap Act claim because “Plaintiff does
16   not plausibly allege any communication was intercepted ‘while in transit.’” (Mot. 14:11–
17   15:3.) The law defines “intercept” as “the aural or other acquisition of the contents of any
18   wire, electronic, or oral communication through the use of any electronic, mechanical, or
19   other device.” 18 U.S.C. § 2510(4). The statute does not define “acquisition,” but the
20   Ninth Circuit—looking at the term’s “ordinary meaning”—defines it as the “act of
21   acquiring, or coming into possession of.” United States v. Smith, 155 F.3d 1051, 1055 n.7
22   (9th Cir. 1998). Further, for a communication to be intercepted, it must have been
23   “acquired during transmission, not while it is in electronic storage.” Konop v. Hawaiian
24   Airlines, Inc., 302 F.3d 868, 878 (9th Cir. 2002).
25         Plaintiff alleges the tracking software employed by Health Warehouse intercepted
26   her electronic communications “contemporaneously” and “simultaneously” and redirected
27   them to third-parties, including Meta. (TAC ¶¶ 54, 153, 155–56.) These allegations
28   plausibly state the interception element. See 18 U.S.C. § 2510(4); cf. Noel v. Hall, 568

                                                  - 14 -
                                                                                            23cv0565
     Case 3:23-cv-00565-BAS-DEB        Document 32 Filed 02/13/25       PageID.<pageID>
                                        Page 15 of 26


 1   F.3d 743, 749 (9th Cir. 2009) (reasoning an acquisition occurs “when the contents of a wire
 2   communication are captured or redirected in any way”). Consequently, the Court rejects
 3   this Rule 12(b)(6) challenge.
 4                4.    Consent by a Party to the Communication
 5         Health Warehouse raises another consent argument that is tailored to the Wiretap
 6   Act claim.    Defendant argues it consented to the interceptions as a party to the
 7   communications, which can defeat the claim. (Mot. 13:22–14:10.) The statute provides:
 8         It shall not be unlawful under this chapter for a person not acting under color
           of law to intercept a wire, oral, or electronic communication where such
 9
           person is a party to the communication or where one of the parties to the
10         communication has given prior consent to such interception unless such
           communication is intercepted for the purpose of committing any criminal or
11
           tortious act in violation of the Constitution or laws of the United States or of
12         any State.
13   18 U.S.C. § 2511(2)(d). Hence, the Wiretap Act allows interception where the interception
14   is made by a party to the communication or where one party has consented to the
15   interception. Id. This carveout does not apply, however, where the interceptor acts “for
16   the purpose of” committing any crime or tort in violation of state or federal law. Id.
17         The Complaint alleges Health Warehouse intentionally used software to intercept
18   and acquire the contents of Plaintiff’s communications for advertising and marketing
19   purposes. (TAC ¶ 238.) The only reasonable inference that can be drawn from this
20   allegation is that Health Warehouse consented to the interceptions. And because the
21   Wiretap Act is a one-party consent statute, Health Warehouse is exempt from liability—
22   regardless of whether Plaintiff consented—so long as Health Warehouse did not act “for
23   the purpose of” committing any crime or tort. 18 U.S.C. § 2511(2)(d). Put simply,
24   Plaintiff’s Wiretap Act claim can proceed only if she shows the crime-tort exception
25   applies. See Meta Pixel Healthcare Litig., 647 F. Supp. 3d at 796.
26         The crime-tort exception focuses on whether “the purpose for the interception—its
27   intended use—was criminal or tortious.” Sussman v. Am. Broad. Cos., 186 F.3d 1200,
28   1202 (9th Cir. 1999) (emphasis in original). The plaintiff “must allege that either the

                                                - 15 -
                                                                                          23cv0565
     Case 3:23-cv-00565-BAS-DEB        Document 32 Filed 02/13/25       PageID.<pageID>
                                        Page 16 of 26


 1   ‘primary motivation or a determining factor in [the defendant’s] actions has been to injure
 2   plaintiffs tortiously.’” Meta Pixel Healthcare Litig., 647 F. Supp. 3d at 797 (quoting
 3   Brown, 525 F. Supp. 3d at 1067).
 4         Several decisions from the Northern District of California have reasoned “that the
 5   crime-tort exception to the Wiretap Act is inapplicable where the defendant’s primary
 6   motivation was to make money, not to injure plaintiffs tortiously.” Id.; see also Rodriguez
 7   v. Google LLC, No. 20-CV-04688-RS, 2021 WL 2026726, at *6 n.8 (N.D. Cal. May 21,
 8   2021) (finding the exception inapplicable where Google’s alleged interceptions occurred
 9   with the consent of application developers and were financially motivated); In re Google
10   Inc. Gmail Litig., No. 13-MD-02430-LHK, 2014 WL 1102660, at *18 (N.D. Cal. Mar. 18,
11   2014) (Koh, J.) (reasoning the tort or crime exception cannot apply where the interceptor’s
12   “purpose has plainly not been to perpetuate torts on millions of Internet users, but to make
13   money” (quoting In re DoubleClick Inc. Priv. Litig., 154 F. Supp. 2d 497, 524 (S.D.N.Y.
14   2001))).
15         The picture is murkier if a defendant is capturing health information on a website.
16   Courts have reached different results. Compare Doe v. Kaiser Found. Health Plan, Inc.,
17   No. 23-CV-02865-EMC, 2024 WL 1589982, at *10 (N.D. Cal. Apr. 11, 2024) (reasoning
18   plaintiffs failed to plead Kaiser had a tortious purpose when it intercepted patients’ health
19   information on its website and mobile applications), and Smith v. Facebook, Inc., 262 F.
20   Supp. 3d 943, 954 (N.D. Cal. 2017) (rejecting argument that Facebook captured protected
21   health information where the plaintiffs visited webpages containing “general health
22   information that is accessible to the public at large”), with Meta Pixel Healthcare Litig.,
23   647 F. Supp. 3d at 797 (noting the exception might apply where health care providers’ use
24   of tracking software gives rise to a tort), and Costco, 2024 WL 4785136, at *6 (applying
25   the exception where Costco intercepted protected health information), and R.C. v.
26   Walgreen Co., 733 F. Supp. 3d 876, 885 (C.D. Cal. 2024) (reasoning the exception applied
27   where the plaintiffs alleged Walgreens intercepted website communications “with criminal
28   and/or tortious intent”).

15         C.     California’s Invasion of Privacy Act
16                1.      Section 631—Eavesdropping
17         Moving beyond the Wiretap Act claim, Defendant contends Plaintiff fails to state a
18   claim under California’s Invasion of Privacy Act (“CIPA”). Plaintiff pleads two CIPA
19   claims. First, in her second cause of action, she alleges Health Warehouse violated
20   California Penal Code section 631 (“Section 631”). (TAC ¶¶ 240–60.)
21         Section 631 is analogous to the Wiretap Act claim and prohibits surreptitious
22   eavesdropping. See Cal. Penal Code § 631(a). Therefore, the analysis for the two claims
23   overlaps. E.g., Brodsky v. Apple Inc., 445 F. Supp. 3d 110, 127 (N.D. Cal. 2020).
24         The overlap between these claims covers many of Health Warehouse’s arguments
25   under Section 631. (See Mot. 11:17–12:20; 12:21–13:13; 17:12–19:11 (arguing Plaintiff
26   fails to allege an intentional interception of a communication in transit for the Section 631
27   claim).) The Court rejects these arguments for the same reasons explained above for the

 1   Wiretap Act claim. Nevertheless, Health Warehouse offers several standalone reasons for
 2   dismissing the Section 631 claim.
 3                         i.      Applicability to Internet Communications
 4         Health Warehouse contends Section 631 “does not apply to internet
 5   communications” because the statute has a clause addressing telephone wires. (Mot. 17:1–
 6   11.) Defendant’s argument ignores the other clause in the statute:
 7         Section 631 has two clauses: It punishes (1) persons who tap telegraph or
           telephone wires, lines, cables, and instruments and (2) persons who attempt to
 8
           learn in an unauthorized manner the contents of communications passing over
 9         any wires, lines, and cables. The first clause applies only to “telegraph and
           telephone” wires, lines, cables or instruments, while the second clause applies
10
           to “any wire, line, or cable.” As a result, courts have concluded that the first
11         clause does not apply to internet connections, while the second clause does.
12   Greenley v. Kochava, Inc., 684 F. Supp. 3d 1024, 1051 (S.D. Cal. 2023) (citation omitted)
13   (collecting cases).        The second clause is plainly the one at issue here, and Health
14   Warehouse’s argument that Section 631 does not apply to electronic communications fails.
15                         ii.     Extension of the Website
16         Health Warehouse contends the tracking software it employed is an “extension” of
17   its website that harvests data for its own use, so there is no forbidden third-party
18   eavesdropper under Section 631. (Mot. 16:1–28.) “It is never a secret to one party to a
19   conversation that the other party is listening to the conversation; only a third party can
20   listen secretly to a private conversation.” Rogers v. Ulrich, 52 Cal. App. 3d 894, 899
21   (1975). Thus, CIPA—like the Wiretap Act—contains an exemption from liability for a
22   party to the communication. Facebook Tracking Litig., 956 F.3d at 607 (citing Warden v.
23   Kahn, 99 Cal. App. 3d 805, 811 (1979)). So, the issue is whether Defendant’s reliance on
24   tracking software introduces a forbidden third-party eavesdropper to its electronic
25   “conversations” with Plaintiff.
26         Dueling caselaw exists for Health Warehouse’s position. Under the first line of
27   cases, software vendors can be viewed as “‘extension[s]’ of the websites that employ them,
28   and thus not third parties within the meaning of the statute.” Javier v. Assurance IQ, LLC,

                                                                                          23cv0565

 1   649 F. Supp. 3d 891, 899 (N.D. Cal. 2023) (citing Graham v. Noom, Inc., 533 F. Supp. 3d
 2   823, 832 (N.D. Cal. 2021)). These cases reason that the tracking defendant provides a tool,
 3   much like a tape recorder, and therefore is a participant in the conversation, not an
 4   eavesdropper. Graham, 533 F. Sup. 2d at 832–833. The other cases reach the opposite
 5   result: they find third parties that capture and interpret users’ data can run afoul of Section
 6   631. Javier, 649 F. Supp. 3d at 899 (collecting cases).
 7         In Javier, Judge Breyer found the argument advanced by defendants like Health
 8   Warehouse raises two problems. First, the argument relies on “the intentions and usage of
 9   the prospective third party,” but the relevant portion of Section 631 does not touch on these
10   items. Id. Second, when the California Supreme Court applied Section 631, it emphasized
11   the statute “concerns ‘the right to control the nature and extent of the firsthand
12   dissemination of [one’s] statements,’” and the court’s decision did not rely on the third
13   parties’ “intentions or the use to which they put the information they obtained.” Id. at 900
14   (quoting Ribas v. Clark, 38 Cal. 3d 355, 361 (1985)).
15         This reasoning is persuasive. Plaintiff alleges third-party data harvesters received
16   her communications, and those parties had the capability to use the data for their own
17   benefit. (TAC ¶¶ 135–36, 138–40.) The tracking software Defendant employed thus
18   “extends beyond the ordinary function of a tape recorder” and is more akin to “an
19   eavesdropper standing outside the door.” See Yoon v. Lululemon USA, Inc., 549 F. Supp.
20   3d 1073, 1081 (C.D. Cal. 2021). Consequently, Health Warehouse’s argument that the
21   tracking software was simply an “extension” of its website is unpersuasive. See, e.g.,
22   Javier, 649 F. Supp. 3d at 900–01.

27   transit or passing over any wire, line, or cable, or is being sent from, or received at any
28   place within this state.” Cal. Penal Code § 631.

14   information” is subject to liability under the CMIA.           Id. § 56.101(a).       “Medical

16   provider of health care . . . regarding a patient’s medical history.” Id. § 56.05.
17         Health Warehouse contends the CMIA claim is implausible because Plaintiff “fails
18   to allege that she was a ‘patient.’” (Mot. 24:7–16.) The CMIA defines a “patient” as “a
19   natural person, whether or not still living, who received health care services from a provider
20   of health care and to whom medical information pertains.” Cal. Civ. Code § 56.05(m).
21   Defendant also contends Plaintiff does not allege Health Warehouse disclosed “medical
22   information” under the CMIA or that the “medical information” was “actually viewed” to
23   give rise to a CMIA claim. (Mot. 24:17–26:21 (citing Sutter Health v. Superior Ct., 227
24   Cal. App. 4th 1546, 1555 (2014)).)
25         The Court is unconvinced that Plaintiff states a viable CMIA claim. In Opposition,
26   Plaintiff points to her allegation that she is a “patient of Defendant, as defined in Civil Code
27   § 56.05(k),” and “Defendant maintains personal medical information of Plaintiff” within
28   the meaning of the CMIA. (TAC ¶¶ 263–64.) Yet a formulaic recitation of the elements

12         F.     California Consumer Privacy Act
13         Last, Defendant asks the Court to dismiss Plaintiff’s fourth claim under the
14   California Consumer Privacy Act of 2018 (“CCPA”). The CCPA calls for enforcement
15   by the California Attorney General, and it allows for a limited private right of action in the
16   event of a security breach. See Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.150. Further, the CCPA only applies
17   to businesses that meet one or more “thresholds,” including having an annual gross revenue
18   in excess of $25 million or annually sharing “the personal information of 100,000 or more
19   consumers or households.” Id. § 1798.140(d).
20         Health Warehouse highlights that the Complaint does not include factual allegations
21   showing one of the CCPA’s thresholds applies to Defendant. (Mot. 27:3–28:19.) Plaintiff
22   responds that she is pleading the CCPA claim in the event that Health Warehouse is not
23   subject to the CMIA. (Opp’n 29:3–12.) Even so, she must still plausibly allege the CCPA
24   applies here, and she does not do so. Hence, the Court dismisses the CCPA claim with
25   leave to amend.

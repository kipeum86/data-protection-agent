                                   5   II.    LEGAL STANDARD
                                   6          A defendant may remove “any civil action brought in a State court of which the
                                   7   district courts . . . have original jurisdiction.” 28 U.S.C. § 1441(a). District courts have
                                   8   federal question jurisdiction over civil actions that “aris[e] under the Constitution, laws, or
                                   9   treaties of the United States.” Id. § 1331. A case can “aris[e] under” federal law in two
                                  10   ways. First, “a case arises under federal law when federal law creates the cause of action
                                  11   asserted.” Gunn v. Minton, 568 U.S. 251, 257 (2013). Second, “federal jurisdiction over a
                                  12   state law claim will lie if a federal issue is: (1) necessarily raised, (2) actually disputed, (3)



 United States District Court
                                  13   substantial, and (4) capable of resolution in federal court without disrupting the federal-
                                  14   state balance approved by Congress.” Id. at 258 (citing Grable & Sons Metal Prods., Inc.
                                  15   v. Darue Eng’g & Mfg., 545 U.S. 308, 313–14 (2005)).



Northern District of California
                                  16          Federal district courts have supplemental jurisdiction over related claims, 28 U.S.C.
                                  17   § 1367(a), so a defendant may remove where there is federal jurisdiction over a single
                                  18   claim. Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah Servs., Inc., 545 U.S. 546, 562–63 (2005). Where
                                  19   “the case stated by the initial pleading is not removable, a notice of removal may be filed
                                  20   within thirty days after receipt by the defendant . . . of a copy of an amended pleading,
                                  21   motion, order or other paper from which it may first be ascertained that the case is one
                                  22   which is or has become removable.” 28 U.S.C. 1446(b)(3).
                                  23          There is a “strong presumption” against removal jurisdiction, and courts “strictly
                                  24   construe the removal statute against removal.” Gaus v. Miles, Inc., 980 F.2d 564, 566 (9th
                                  25   Cir. 1992). This “means that the defendant always has the burden of establishing that
                                  26   removal is proper.” Hunter v. Philip Morris USA, 582 F.3d 1039, 1042 (9th Cir. 2009)
                                  27   (citation and internal quotation marks omitted). Any doubt regarding the legitimacy of the
                                  28   removal favors remanding the case to state court. Id.

                                       III.   DISCUSSION
                                   1
                                              This order addresses: (A) CAFA jurisdiction generally; (B) Plaintiff’s argument that
                                   2
                                       the Court must remand this matter under CAFA’s “mandatory home state exception”; (C)
                                   3
                                       Plaintiff’s alternative argument that the Court should exercise its discretion to remand
                                   4
                                       under CAFA’s “discretionary home state exception”; and (D) the issues of jurisdictional
                                   5
                                       discovery and leave to amend.
                                   6
                                              A.     CAFA Jurisdiction
                                   7
                                              CAFA provides that federal district courts shall have original jurisdiction over civil
                                   8
                                       class actions where the matter in controversy exceeds five million dollars and “any
                                   9
                                       member of a class of plaintiffs is a citizen of a State different from any defendant[.]” 28
                                  10
                                       U.S.C. § 1332(d)(2). “Congress enacted CAFA in 2005 to ‘curb perceived abuses of the
                                  11
                                       class action device which, in the view of CAFA’s proponents, had often been used to
                                  12



 United States District Court
                                       litigate multistate or even national class actions in state courts.’” Corber v. Xanodyne
                                  13
                                       Pharm., Inc., 771 F.3d 1218, 1222 (9th Cir. 2014) (en banc) (quoting Tanoh v. Dow Chem.
                                  14
                                       Co., 561 F.3d 945, 952 (9th Cir. 2009)). Thus, “CAFA was intended to strongly favor
                                  15
                                       federal jurisdiction over interstate class actions.” King v. Great Am. Chicken Corp, Inc.,


Northern District of California
                                  16
                                       903 F.3d 875, 878 (9th Cir. 2018).
                                  17
                                              “Congress provided exceptions to CAFA jurisdiction, however, to allow truly
                                  18
                                       intrastate class actions to be heard in state court.” Adams v. W. Marine Prod., Inc., 958
                                  19
                                       F.3d 1216, 1220 (9th Cir. 2020) (alteration in original). The two exceptions that are
                                  20
                                       relevant here are (1) the “mandatory home state exception” and (2) the “discretionary
                                  21
                                       home state exception.”
                                  22
                                                     1.     The “Mandatory Home State Exception”
                                  23
                                              The “mandatory home state exception” to CAFA provides, in relevant part, that a
                                  24
                                       district court must decline to exercise jurisdiction over a class action in which “greater than
                                  25
                                       two-thirds of the members of all proposed plaintiff classes in the aggregate are citizens of
                                  26
                                       the State in which the action was originally filed.” 28 U.S.C. § 1332(d)(4)(A).
                                  27
                                  28
                                                                                     4

                                                     2.     The “Discretionary Home State Exception”
                                   1
                                              CAFA’s “discretionary home state exception” provides that “a district court may, in
                                   2
                                       the interests of justice and looking at the totality of the circumstances, decline to exercise
                                   3
                                       jurisdiction . . . over a class action in which greater than one-third but less than two-thirds
                                   4
                                       of the members of all proposed plaintiff classes in the aggregate and the primary
                                   5
                                       defendants are citizens of the State in which the action was originally filed.” 28 U.S.C.
                                   6
                                       1332(d)(3). CAFA outlines six factors for a district court to consider in deciding whether
                                   7
                                       to decline jurisdiction under this discretionary home state exception. 28 U.S.C. §
                                   8
                                       1332(d)(3)(A)–(F).
                                   9
                                              B.     Plaintiff has not shown that greater than two-thirds of the proposed
                                  10                 class are citizens of California.
                                  11          There appears to be no dispute here that at least one defendant is a defendant (i)
                                  12   from whom significant relief is sought by members of the plaintiff class, (ii) whose alleged



 United States District Court
                                  13   conduct forms a significant basis for the claims asserted by the proposed plaintiff class,
                                  14   and (iii) who is a citizen of the State in which the action was originally filed. See 28
                                  15   U.S.C. § 1332(d)(4)(A). Nor is there any dispute that the alleged principal injuries



Northern District of California
                                  16   resulting from the alleged conduct were incurred in the State in which the action was
                                  17   originally filed, and that during the 3-year period preceding the filing of that class action,
                                  18   no other class action has been filed asserting the same or similar factual allegations against
                                  19   any of the defendants on behalf of the same or other persons. See id. Thus, the Court’s
                                  20   analysis as to the mandatory home state exception will center on whether Plaintiff has
                                  21   demonstrated that two-thirds of the members of the putative class are California citizens.
                                  22          To qualify for the mandatory home state exception, Plaintiff bears the burden of
                                  23   proving that greater than two-thirds of putative class members are California citizens. 28
                                  24   U.S.C. § 1332(d)(4)(A)(i)(I); see Adams, 958 F.3d at 1221. “To meet this burden, the
                                  25   moving party must provide ‘some facts from which the district court may make findings
                                  26   regarding class members’ citizenship.’” Brinkley v. Monterey Fin. Servs., Inc., 873 F.3d
                                  27   1118, 1121 (9th Cir. 2017) (quoting Mondragon v. Capital One Auto Fin., 736 F.3d 880,
                                  28   884 (9th Cir. 2013)). “To be a citizen of a state, a natural person must first be a citizen of
                                                                                      5
                                       Case 3:24-cv-01797-CRB          Document 15        Filed 05/23/24    Page 6 of 11




                                   1   the United States.” Kanter v. Warner-Lambert Co., 265 F.3d 853, 857 (9th Cir. 2001).
                                   2   “The natural person’s state citizenship is then determined by her state of domicile, not her
                                   3   state of residence. A person’s domicile is her permanent home, where she resides with the
                                   4   intention to remain or to which she intends to return.” Id. A district court makes factual
                                   5   findings regarding jurisdiction under a preponderance of the evidence standard.
                                   6   Mondragon, 736 F.3d at 884. Although such a finding must be based on more than
                                   7   “guesswork,” the Ninth Circuit has cautioned that the burden of proof on a plaintiff
                                   8   “should not be exceptionally difficult to bear.” Id. at 884, 886. Even under this lenient
                                   9   standard, Plaintiff did not meet her burden here.
                                  10          The first stumbling block for Plaintiff is that the size of the class is unknown. “In
                                  11   order to determine whether two-thirds of class members are California citizens, we must
                                  12   first determine the size of the class as a whole.” Brinkley, 873 F.3d at 1121. Determining



 United States District Court
                                  13   the denominator of the class size is crucial to calculating the fraction of California citizens
                                  14   contained in the class.
                                  15          Here, Plaintiff’s class consists of “all California residents who accessed the Website



Northern District of California
                                  16   in California and had their IP addresses collected by the Trackers.” Compl. ¶ 94. Plaintiff
                                  17   acknowledges that the number of people in this class could “amount to thousands, if not
                                  18   millions of persons.” Id. ¶ 96. But Plaintiff offers no evidence to ground the class size in
                                  19   anything more specific than “thousands, if not millions.” Id. This compares unfavorably
                                  20   even to Brinkley, a case in which the Ninth Circuit vacated a motion to remand because
                                  21   the putative class size was undefined. See Brinkley, 873 F.3d at 1118, 1122. In that case,
                                  22   the plaintiff presented evidence of residency for at least a portion of the class, but failed to
                                  23   produce evidence with regard to a class subgroup. Id. at 1122. Despite the partial
                                  24   evidence, the court vacated the motion to remand because the lack of residency evidence
                                  25   for the subgroup rendered the class indefinite. Id. In this case, there is not even partial
                                  26   evidence on the total class size. “Absent ‘some facts in evidence’ regarding the size of the
                                  27   entire class, the district court cannot determine whether two-thirds of all class members are
                                  28   California citizens.” Id. (quoting Mondragon, 736 F.3d at 884). Thus, on this score alone,

                                   1   Plaintiff “has not met her burden to show that the home-state controversy exception
                                   2   applies.” Id.
                                   3          Next, the slim evidence that Plaintiff does offer is not sufficient to establish that
                                   4   two-thirds of the putative class are California citizens. To prove citizenship, Plaintiff
                                   5   points to evidence that “as of February 2023, 26.7 million of the 39 million people living

                                   7   California citizens. “A jurisdictional finding of fact should be based on more than
                                   8   guesswork.” Mondragon, 736 F.3d at 884. The evidence offered by Plaintiff here amounts
                                   9   to mere guesswork. While it is “[s]ensible guesswork,” given that Defendant is a

                                  16          The Ninth Circuit has vacated motions to remand based on far more tailored
                                  17   evidence. For example, in King v. Great American Chicken Corp, Inc., the district court
                                  18   granted remand based on a stipulation that “at least 67% of the last-known addresses [of
                                  19   class members] are in California.” 903 F.3d at 877. The Ninth Circuit vacated the motion
                                  20   to remand because the stipulation failed to account for the likelihood that “at least some . .
                                  21   . in the group [had] moved out of California” at the time of removal, that “at least a few
                                  22   [class members] were citizens of other states even if they temporarily had a residential
                                  23   address in California, such as an out-of-state student working while attending college in
                                  24   California,” or that at least “some putative class members were not United States citizens.”
                                  25   Id. at 879.
                                  26          “The problem” with the King stipulation, as with Plaintiff’s voter eligibility
                                  27   estimation in this case, “is that this impression rest[ed] on guesswork.” Id. at 880. Here,
                                  28   as in King, “[t]here [is] no evidence to support a factual finding that the proportion of

                                   1   California citizens was greater than two-thirds.” Id. What is more, the evidence offered
                                   2   here is unrelated to the putative class. As explored above, the percentage of Californians
                                   3   who are eligible to vote tells the Court nothing about which of those Californians (a) are
                                   4   citizens, (b) accessed the Website, and (c) were in California at the time they were tracked.

                                  13   the Ninth Circuit to reject the stipulation in King. Id.

                                  21   Ninth Circuit acknowledged that last known addresses are an imperfect proxy for

                                              C.     Plaintiff has also not shown that greater than one-third of the proposed
                                   1                 class are California citizens.
                                   2          The above analysis applies to the discretionary home state exception as well. The
                                   3   indeterminate class size remains an issue. See Brinkley, 873 F.3d at 1121 (“In order to
                                   4   determine whether two-thirds of class members are California citizens, we must first
                                   5   determine the size of the class as a whole.”). There is also a lack of evidence from which
                                   6   the Court can conclude that at least one-third of the putative class are California citizens.
                                   7   The evidence presented here is far weaker than the last known addresses that were
                                   8   sufficient in Adams. See Adams, 958 F.3d at 1223 (holding that the plaintiff met their
                                   9   burden where “more than 90% of class members had last known mailing addresses in
                                  10   California—a percentage far greater than the one-third (or roughly, 33%) required for
                                  11   remand.”). Because Plaintiff has failed to meet her burden, the Court need not apply the
                                  12   discretionary factors for remand found in 28 U.S.C. § 1332(d)(3)(A)–(F).

                                  13          D.     Jurisdictional Discovery and Leave to Amend

                                  16   and renew her motion to remand. Mot. to Remand at 8. The Ninth Circuit has repeatedly

 1       II.      LEGAL STANDARD
 2             “A district court is vested with broad discretion to permit or deny discovery.” Laub
 3    v. U.S. Dep’t of Interior, 342 F.3d 1080, 1093 (9th Cir. 2003). Unless otherwise limited by
 4    court order, the scope of discovery under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure is as
 5    follows:
 6             Parties may obtain discovery regarding any nonprivileged matter that is
               relevant to any party’s claim or defense and proportional to the needs of the
 7
               case, considering the importance of the issues at stake in the action, the
 8             amount in controversy, the parties’ relative access to relevant information, the
               parties’ resources, the importance of the discovery in resolving the issues, and
 9
               whether the burden or expense of the proposed discovery outweighs its likely
10             benefit. Information within this scope of discovery need not be admissible in
               evidence to be discoverable.
11
12    Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(b)(1). Courts must limit the frequency or extent of discovery if it
13    determines that:
14             (i) the discovery sought is unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or can be
15             obtained from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome,
               or less expensive; (ii) the party seeking discovery has had ample opportunity
16             to obtain the information by discovery in the action; or (iii) the proposed
17             discovery is outside the scope permitted by Rule 26(b)(1).
18    Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(b)(2)(C).
19             “[A] party may move for an order compelling disclosure of discovery.” Fed. R. Civ.
20    P. 37(a)(1). “The party seeking to compel discovery has the burden of establishing that its
21    request satisfies the relevancy requirement of Rule 26(b)(1).” La. Pac. Corp. v. Money
22    Mkt. 1 Inst. Inv. Dealer, 285 F.R.D. 481, 485 (N.D. Cal. 2012). “The party who resists
23    discovery has the burden to show discovery should not be allowed, and has the burden of
24    clarifying, explaining, and supporting its objections.” Blankenship v. Hearst Corp., 519
25    F.2d 418, 429 (9th Cir. 1975).

 9              B.     Pre-Certification Discovery
10          “The propriety of a class action cannot be determined in some cases without
11    discovery, as for example, where discovery is necessary to determine the existence of a
12    class or set of subclasses.” Kamm v. Cal. City Dev. Co., 509 F.2d 205, 210 (9th Cir. 1975);
13    see also Vinole v. Countrywide Home Loans, Inc., 571 F.3d 935, 942 (9th Cir. 2009)
14    (“Although a party seeking class certification is not always entitled to discovery on the
15    class certification issue, . . . often the pleadings alone will not resolve the question of class
16    certification and . . . . some discovery will be warranted.”); Doninger v. Pac. Nw. Bell, Inc.,
17    564 F.2d 1304, 1313 (9th Cir. 1977) (“[T]he necessary antecedent to the presentation of
18    evidence is, in most cases, enough discovery to obtain the material, especially when the
19    information is within the sole possession of the defendant.”). “Although in some cases a
20    district court should allow discovery to aid the determination of whether a class action is
21    maintainable, the plaintiff bears the burden of advancing a prima facie showing that the
22    class action requirements of Fed. R. Civ. P. 23 are satisfied or that discovery is likely to
23    produce substantiation of the class allegations.” Mantolete v. Bolger, 767 F.2d 1416, 1424
24    (9th Cir. 1985), as amended (Aug. 27, 1985); see also Doninger, 564 F.2d at 1313
25    (“[W]here the plaintiffs fail to make even a prima facie showing of Rule 23’s prerequisites
26    . . . . the burden is on the plaintiff to demonstrate that discovery measures are likely to
27    produce persuasive information substantiating the class action allegations.”).

 1              C.     Relevance
 2           The Court finds that the contact information sought in RFP No. 11 is relevant. As an
 3    initial matter, the Court notes that it is common in class actions to produce names,
 4    addresses, and telephone numbers of putative class members. Artis v. Deere & Co., 276
 5    F.R.D. 348, 352 (N.D. Cal. 2011); Faraji v. Target Corp., 2017 WL 8292781, at *4 (C.D.
 6    Cal. April 28, 2017). As noted above, relevancy for the purpose of discovery encompasses
 7    “any party’s claim or defense.” Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(b)(2). Here, Plaintiff argues that it “is
 8    entitled to the contact information of the putative class members because it is highly
 9    relevant to both class certification and the claims and defenses of the action.” MTC at 4.
10    Plaintiff points to the specific affirmative defenses that it seeks to gather evidence about.
11    Id. at 7-8.
12           Defendant’s argument that Plaintiff “does not come close to meeting his burden of
13    establishing that the contact information for thousands of individuals is relevant” is
14    unpersuasive. Oppo. at 6. Defendant relies largely on Goro v. Flowers Foods, Inc., 334
15    F.R.D. 275, 2018 WL 3956018, at *8 (S.D. Cal. Aug. 17, 2018) in support of its argument.
16    Goro involved a claim brought by plaintiffs who worked as distributors against defendant,
17    a baked good production company, alleging that they had been misclassified as contractors
18    rather than employees. Id. at 279. In Goro, plaintiffs brought a motion to compel the names
19    and contact information for individuals who performed direct store delivery services and
20    entered into a form distributor agreement with the defendant. Id. at 285. The court found
21    that plaintiffs met their burden to establish that individuals and entities that contracted with
22    defendant may possess information relevant to the claims and defenses in that case. Id.
23    However, the court in Goro found that plaintiffs failed to indicate what relevant
24    information, if any, individuals who did not sign distributor agreements with defendant
25    may have. Id.
26           Goro is inapposite to the facts in our case. Here, Plaintiff alleges that a data breach
27    caused by a theft of servers from Defendant’s office resulted in 5,725 individuals’
28    information being compromised. MTC at 4; ECF No. 26, ¶ 2. The putative class is

                                                     7
                                                                                           21cv31-H-LL
     Case 3:21-cv-00031-H-LL Document 48 Filed 10/12/21 PageID.<pageID> Page 8 of 11



 1    comprised of these 5,725 individuals and, unlike in Goro, all putative plaintiffs were
 2    Defendant’s banking customers. Plaintiff seeks the contact information of the 5,725
 3    putative class members “because it is highly relevant to both class certification and the
 4    claims and defenses of the action.” MTC at 4. The Court agrees with Plaintiff that the
 5    contact information is relevant to both class certification and the claims and defenses of
 6    the action. In sum, the Court finds that Plaintiff has met his burden to show that class
 7    contact information is relevant at this stage in the litigation. See Salgado v. O’Lakes, 2014
 8    WL 7272784, at *11 (E.D. Cal. Dec. 18, 2014) (“Ultimately, ‘a court maintains a wide
 9    latitude in deciding whether contact information of putative class members should be
10    produced prior to class certification…’”) (internal citation omitted). Accordingly, the Court
11    concludes that the disputed discovery is relevant under Rule 26.
12             D.     Proportionality
13          Defendant argues that “Plaintiff’s request is not proportional to the needs of this
14    case.” Oppo. at 8. Defendant states that Plaintiff “ignores completely his burden of
15    establishing that the information is proportional. Nowhere does Plaintiff explain why
16    contact information for thousands of individuals is proportional to the needs of this case.”
17    Id. at 4. The Court finds that Defendant is correct that Plaintiff fails to address why the
18    production of all 5,725 putative class members’ contact information would be proportional
19    in this case. See MTC.
20          Defendant states in the Opposition that “if this Court is inclined to permit discovery
21    of contact information in this case, Defendant respectfully requests that the Court permit
22    discovery for only a sampling of putative class members (250) and implement additional
23    protective measures governing Plaintiff’s counsel’s contact with putative class members.”
24    Oppo. at 10. Notably, Defendant does not explain how it arrived at the proposed sampling
25    of 250 putative class members. Defendant possesses the burden of supporting its
26    objections, but fails to show why producing more than the proposed sample of 250 putative
27    class members is not proportional or would be unduly burdensome.

 7              E.     Privacy
 8          The California Supreme Court established a framework for assessing privacy claims
 9    in which “[t]he party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy
10    interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and
11    a   threatened     intrusion    that    is    serious.”    Williams     v.    Superior     Ct.,
12    3 Cal. 5th at 552 (citing Hill v. Nat'l Collegiate Athletic Assn., 7 Cal. 4th 1, 35-37 (1994)).
13    “The party seeking information may raise in response whatever legitimate and important
14    countervailing interests disclosure serves, while the party seeking protection may identify
15    feasible alternatives that serve the same interests or protective measures that would
16    diminish the loss of privacy.” Id. The court then balances these considerations. Id. (citing
17    Hill v. Nat'l Collegiate Athletic Assn., 7 Cal. 4th at 37–40).
18          The Court has balanced the need of the information against the putative class
19    members’ privacy interests and concludes that the information sought by Plaintiff should
20    be produced. In support of Defendant’s privacy objection, Defendant argues that “any
21    relevance is outweighed by the putative class members’ privacy interest.” Oppo. at 8.
22    Defendant fails to explain how the disclosure of the contact information for a sampling of
23    putative class members would be harmful to them, or why any such harm would not be
24    mitigated by production of this information subject to a protective order. Accordingly, the
25    Court finds that the requested discovery (contact information for a sampling of the putative
26    class members) does not constitute a serious invasion of privacy. See, e.g., Haghyayeghi
27    v. Guess?, Inc., 168 F. Supp. 3d 1277, 1281 (S.D. Cal. March 10, 2016) (“disclosing names
28    and contact information does not constitute a serious invasion of privacy and is

 1     commonplace in class actions.”).
 2           Additionally, there is a protective order entered in this case [ECF No. 35], which
 3     courts have found to be sufficient to protect the privacy of putative class members. See,
 4     e.g., Hayghyayeghi, 168 F. Supp. 3d 1281; see also Coleman v. Jenny Craig, Inc., 2013
 5     WL 2896884, at *10 (S.D. Cal. June 12, 2013) (finding that “‘the parties can craft a
 6     protective order that limits the use of any contact information to the parties in this ligation
 7     and protects it from disclosure.’”). Here, because the protective order was entered prior to
 8     the issuance of this discovery order, the Court orders the following additional measures in
 9     Plaintiff’s communications with the putative class members. First, Plaintiff’s counsel shall
10     identify themselves as Plaintiff’s counsel in this putative class action. Second, Plaintiff’s
11     counsel shall inform the contacted putative class member that they have the right not to
12     talk to or communicate with counsel, and if they decline to talk or communicate, then
13     counsel shall terminate the conversation and not contact them again. Third, Plaintiff’s
14     counsel shall inform each contacted putative class member that the Court compelled
15     Defendant U.S. Bank to disclose the putative class member’s contact information, and that
16     the contact information was provided solely for this lawsuit and cannot be distributed for
17     other uses.

 1        IV.   CONCLUSION
 2           For the reasons set forth above, the Court GRANTS IN PART AND DENIES IN

                                       III.    LEGAL STANDARD
                                  17
                                               A.      Rule 12(b)(1)
                                  18
                                               A motion to dismiss under Rule 12(b)(1) tests the subject matter jurisdiction of the court.
                                  19
                                       See Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(1). If a plaintiff lacks Article III standing to bring a suit, the federal
                                  20
                                       court lacks subject matter jurisdiction, and the suit must be dismissed under Rule 12(b)(1).
                                  21
                                       Cetacean Cmty. v. Bush, 386 F.3d 1169, 1174 (9th Cir. 2004). “A Rule 12(b)(1) jurisdictional
                                  22
                                       attack may be facial or factual. In a facial attack, the challenger asserts that the allegations
                                  23
                                       contained in a complaint are insufficient on their face to invoke federal jurisdiction. By contrast,
                                  24
                                       in a factual attack, the challenger disputes the truth of the allegations that, by themselves, would
                                  25
                                       otherwise invoke federal jurisdiction.” Safe Air for Everyone v. Meyer, 373 F.3d 1035, 1039 (9th
                                  26
                                       Cir. 2004) (citation omitted). In resolving a facial attack, the court assumes that the allegations are
                                  27
                                       true and draws all reasonable inferences in the plaintiff’s favor. Wolfe v. Strankman, 392 F.3d
                                  28
                                                                                           3
                                        Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST          Document 40            Filed 07/14/25   Page 4 of 26




                                   1   358, 362 (9th Cir. 2004). A court addressing a facial attack must confine its inquiry to the

                                   2   allegations in the complaint. See Savage v. Glendale Union High Sch., Dist. No. 205, Maricopa

                                   3   Cnty., 343 F.3d 1036, 1039 n.2 (9th Cir. 2003).

                                   4          B.      Rule 12(b)(2)

                                   5          Under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(2), the court must dismiss an action when it

                                   6   does not have personal jurisdiction over the defendant. Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(2). “In opposition to

                                   7   a defendant’s motion to dismiss for lack of personal jurisdiction, the plaintiff bears the burden of

                                   8   establishing that jurisdiction is proper.” Boschetto v. Hansing, 539 F.3d 1011, 1015 (9th Cir.

                                   9   2008). Where “the motion is based on written materials rather than an evidentiary hearing, ‘the

                                  10   plaintiff need only make a prima facie showing of jurisdictional facts.’” Schwarzenegger v. Fred

                                  11   Martin Motor Co., 374 F.3d 797, 800 (9th Cir. 2004) (quoting Sher v. Johnson, 911 F.2d 1357,

                                  12   1361 (9th Cir. 1990)). “Although the plaintiff cannot simply rest on the bare allegations of its


 United States District Court
                                  13   complaint, uncontroverted allegations in the complaint must be taken as true. Conflicts between

                                  14   parties over statements contained in affidavits must be resolved in the plaintiff’s favor.” Id.

                                  15   (internal quotation marks and citations omitted).



Northern District of California
                                  16          “Federal courts ordinarily follow state law in determining the bounds of their jurisdiction

                                  17   over persons.” Daimler AG v. Bauman, 571 U.S. 117, 125 (2014). “California’s long-arm statute

                                  18   allows the exercise of personal jurisdiction to the full extent permissible under the U.S.

                                  19   Constitution.” Id. Because the applicable state “statute is coextensive with federal due process

                                  20   requirements, the jurisdictional analyses under state law and federal due process are the same.”

                                  21   Schwarzenegger, 374 F.3d at 800–01.

                                  22          Due process permits a court to exercise personal jurisdiction over a defendant only when

                                  23   “the defendant has sufficient minimum contacts with the forum state such that the maintenance of

                                  24   the suit does not offend traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.” Impossible Foods

                                  25   Inc. v. Impossible X LLC, 80 F.4th 1079, 1086 (9th Cir. 2023) (internal quotation marks and

                                  26   citation omitted)). “The strength of contacts required depends on which of the two categories of

                                  27   personal jurisdiction a litigant invokes: specific jurisdiction or general jurisdiction.” Ranza v.

                                  28   Nike, Inc., 793 F.3d 1059, 1068 (9th Cir. 2015).
                                                                                           4
                                        Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST           Document 40          Filed 07/14/25     Page 5 of 26




                                   1          “A court with general jurisdiction may hear any claim against that defendant, even if all the

                                   2   incidents underlying the claim occurred in a different State.” Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v.

                                   3   Superior Court, 582 U.S. 255, 262 (2017) (emphasis omitted). General jurisdiction over a

                                   4   corporation is appropriate only when the corporation’s contacts with the forum state “are so

                                   5   ‘continuous and systematic’ as to render [it] essentially at home in the forum State.” Daimler AG,

                                   6   571 U.S. at 127 (quoting Goodyear Dunlop Tires Operations, S.A. v. Brown, 564 U.S. 915, 919

                                   7   (2011)). “With respect to a corporation, the place of incorporation and principal place of business

                                   8   are ‘paradig[matic] . . . bases for general jurisdiction.’” Id. at 137 (quoting Goodyear, 564 U.S. at

                                   9   924). If a business is not incorporated in, and does not have its principal place of business in a

                                  10   forum state, “[o]nly in an ‘exceptional case’ will general jurisdiction be available anywhere else.”

                                  11   Martinez v. Aero Caribbean, 764 F.3d 1062, 1070 (9th Cir. 2014) (quoting Daimler AG, 571 U.S.

                                  12   at 139 n.19).


 United States District Court
                                  13          “Specific jurisdiction is very different.” Bristol-Myers, 582 U.S. at 262. For the court to

                                  14   exercise specific jurisdiction, the lawsuit must arise out of or relate to the defendant’s contacts

                                  15   with the forum. Id. “In other words, there must be an affiliation between the forum and the



Northern District of California
                                  16   underlying controversy, principally, [an] activity or an occurrence that takes place in the forum

                                  17   state and is therefore subject to the state’s regulation.” Id. (quoting Goodyear, 564 U.S. at 919).

                                  18          Because California’s long-arm jurisdictional statute authorizes jurisdiction to the fullest

                                  19   extent permitted by the Constitution, see Cal. Code Civ. P. § 410.10, the central question is

                                  20   whether the defendant has “at least ‘minimum contacts’ with [California] such that the exercise of

                                  21   jurisdiction ‘does not offend traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.’”

                                  22   Schwarzenegger, 374 F.3d at 801 (quoting Int’l Shoe Co. v. State of Wash., Off. of Unemployment

                                  23   Comp. & Placement, 326 U.S. 310, 316 (1945)).

                                  24          The Ninth Circuit has established a three-part inquiry, referred to as the minimum contacts

                                  25   test, to determine whether a court has specific personal jurisdiction over a defendant:

                                  26                   (1) The non-resident defendant must purposefully direct his
                                                       activities or consummate some transaction with the forum or
                                  27                   resident thereof; or perform some act by which he purposefully
                                                       avails himself of the privilege of conducting activities in the forum,
                                  28                   thereby invoking the benefits and protections of its laws;
                                                                                          5
                                        Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST            Document 40           Filed 07/14/25     Page 6 of 26




                                   1                   (2) the claim must be one which arises out of or relates to the
                                                       defendant’s forum-related activities; and
                                   2
                                                       (3) the exercise of jurisdiction must comport with fair play and
                                   3                   substantial justice, i.e., it must be reasonable.
                                   4   Id. at 802. “The plaintiff bears the burden of satisfying the first two prongs of the test.” Id. If the

                                   5   plaintiff does so, “the burden then shifts to the defendant to ‘present a compelling case’ that the

                                   6   exercise of jurisdiction would not be reasonable.” Id. (quoting Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz,

                                   7   471 U.S. 462, 476–78 (1985)).

                                   8           C.      Rule 12(b)(6)

                                   9           A complaint must contain “a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the

                                  10   pleader is entitled to relief.” Fed. R. Civ. P. 8(a)(2). “Dismissal under Rule 12(b)(6) is

                                  11   appropriate only where the complaint lacks a cognizable legal theory or sufficient facts to support

                                  12   a cognizable legal theory.” Mendiondo v. Centinela Hosp. Med. Ctr., 521 F.3d 1097, 1104 (9th


 United States District Court
                                  13   Cir. 2008). A complaint need not contain detailed factual allegations, but facts pleaded by a

                                  14   plaintiff “must be enough to raise a right to relief above the speculative level.” Bell Atl. Corp. v.

                                  15   Twombly, 550 U.S. 544, 555 (2007). “To survive a motion to dismiss, a complaint must contain



Northern District of California
                                  16   sufficient factual matter, accepted as true, to state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face.”

                                  17   Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662, 678 (2009) (internal quotation marks and citation omitted). “A

                                  18   claim has facial plausibility when the plaintiff pleads factual content that allows the court to draw

                                  19   the reasonable inference that the defendant is liable for the misconduct alleged.” Id. The Court

                                  20   must “accept all factual allegations in the complaint as true and construe the pleadings in the light

                                  21   most favorable to the nonmoving party.” Knievel, 393 F.3d at 1072. However, the Court is not

                                  22   “required to accept as true allegations that are merely conclusory, unwarranted deductions of fact,

                                  23   or unreasonable inferences.” In re Gilead Scis. Sec. Litig., 536 F.3d 1049, 1055 (9th Cir. 2008)

                                  24   (internal quotation marks and citation omitted).

                                  25   IV.     REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

                                  26           “As a general rule, [courts] ‘may not consider any material beyond the pleadings in ruling

                                  27   on a Rule 12(b)(6) motion.’” United States v. Corinthian Colleges, 655 F.3d 984, 998 (9th Cir.

                                  28   2011) (quoting Lee v. City of Los Angeles, 250 F.3d 668, 688 (9th Cir. 2001)). “When ‘matters
                                                                                            6
                                        Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST          Document 40           Filed 07/14/25   Page 7 of 26




                                   1   outside the pleading are presented to and not excluded by the court,’ the 12(b)(6) motion converts

                                   2   into a motion for summary judgment under Rule 56,” unless those matters satisfy the

                                   3   “incorporation-by-reference doctrine” or the standard for “judicial notice under Federal Rule of

                                   4   Evidence 201.” Khoja v. Orexigen Therapeutics, Inc., 899 F.3d 988, 998 (9th Cir. 2018) (quoting

                                   5   Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(d)). The Ninth Circuit has expressed concern with the practice of “exploiting

                                   6   these procedures improperly to defeat what would otherwise constitute adequately stated claims at

                                   7   the pleading stage.” Id. The Ninth Circuit also cautioned that “[i]f defendants are permitted to

                                   8   present their own version of the facts at the pleading stage—and district courts accept those facts

                                   9   as uncontroverted and true—it becomes near impossible for even the most aggrieved plaintiff to

                                  10   demonstrate a sufficiently ‘plausible’ claim for relief.” Id. at 999.

                                  11          “Judicial notice under Rule 201 permits a court to notice an adjudicative fact if it is ‘not

                                  12   subject to reasonable dispute,’” i.e., the fact “is ‘generally known,’ or ‘can be accurately and


 United States District Court
                                  13   readily determined from sources whose accuracy cannot reasonably be questioned.’” Id. (quoting

                                  14   Fed. R. Evid. 201(b)). “Unlike rule-established judicial notice, incorporation-by-reference is a

                                  15   judicially created doctrine that treats certain documents as though they are part of the complaint



Northern District of California
                                  16   itself.” Id. at 1002. Documents “may be incorporated by reference into a complaint if the plaintiff

                                  17   refers extensively to the document or the document forms the basis of the plaintiff’s claim,”

                                  18   United States v. Ritchie, 342 F.3d 903, 908 (9th Cir. 2003), and “the documents’ authenticity . . . is

                                  19   not contested,” Lee, 250 F.3d at 688 (alteration in original) (quotation marks and citation omitted).

                                  20   “[T]he mere mention of the existence of a document is insufficient to incorporate the contents of a

                                  21   document.” Coto Settlement v. Eisenberg, 593 F.3d 1031, 1038 (9th Cir. 2010).

                                  26   V.     DISCUSSION

                                  27          A.      Standing

                                  28          “Article III of the Constitution confines the federal judicial power to the resolution of
                                                                                          8
                                        Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST           Document 40          Filed 07/14/25     Page 9 of 26




                                   1   ‘Cases’ and ‘Controversies.’” TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez, 594 U.S. 413, 423 (2021). “For there

                                   2   to be a case or controversy under Article III, the plaintiff must have a ‘personal stake’ in the

                                   3   case—in other words, standing.” Id. (quoting Raines v. Byrd, 521 U.S. 811, 819 (1997)). “[T]o

                                   4   establish standing, a plaintiff must show (i) that he suffered an injury in fact that is concrete,

                                   5   particularized, and actual or imminent; (ii) that the injury was likely caused by the defendant, and

                                   6   (iii) that the injury would likely be redressed by judicial relief.” Id. “The party invoking federal

                                   7   jurisdiction bears the burden of establishing these elements.” Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504

                                   8   U.S. 555, 561 (1992). “Where, as here, a case is at the pleading stage, the plaintiff must

                                   9   ‘clearly . . . allege facts demonstrating’ each element.” Spokeo, Inc. v. Robins, 578 U.S. 330, 338

                                  10   (2016) (quoting Warth v. Seldin, 422 U.S. 490, 518 (1975)).

                                  11          Motorola argues that Gabrielli has failed to plead an injury-in-fact—including by failing to

                                  12   identify what specific information was transmitted or what damages he suffered—and thus has no


 United States District Court
                                  13   standing. See ECF No. 12 at 18–19. It also appears to argue that Gabrielli did not suffer any

                                  14   injury-in-fact because he was a “tester” who sought out privacy violations and thus expected his

                                  15   privacy to be invaded. See ECF No. 27 at 9; ECF No. 35 at 3. These arguments fail.



Northern District of California
                                  16          “[V]arious intangible harms can . . . be concrete. Chief among them are injuries with a

                                  17   close relationship to harms traditionally recognized as providing a basis for lawsuits in American

                                  18   courts.” TransUnion, 594 U.S. at 425. An “exact duplicate” is not required, and the Supreme

                                  19   Court has recognized that “intrusion upon seclusion” is included among those traditionally

                                  20   recognized harms. Id. at 2204; see Restatement (Second) of Torts § 652B cmt. b. (1977)

                                  21   (including “tapping . . . telephone wires” as an example of intrusion upon seclusion). In that vein,

                                  22   the Ninth Circuit has written that “[v]iolations of the right to privacy have long been actionable at

                                  23   common law,” and that “[a] right to privacy ‘encompass[es] the individual’s control of

                                  24   information concerning his or her person.’” In re Facebook, Inc. Internet Tracking Litigation, 956

                                  25   F.3d 589, 598 (9th Cir. 2020) (first quoting Patel v. Facebook, 932 F.3d 1264, 1272 (9th Cir.

                                  26   2019); and then quoting Eichenberger v. ESPN, Inc., 876 F.3d 979, 983 (9th Cir. 2017)); see also

                                  27   Restatement (Second) of Torts § 652B cmt. a. (explaining that intrusion upon seclusion “does not

                                  28   depend upon any publicity given to the person whose interest is invaded or to his affairs” but
                                                                                          9
                                       Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST           Document 40         Filed 07/14/25      Page 10 of 26




                                   1   rather “consists solely of an intentional interference with his interest in solicitude or seclusion”).

                                   2   Statutes such as CIPA thus “codify a substantive right to privacy, the violation of which gives rise

                                   3   to a concrete injury sufficient to confer standing.” Id.; accord In re Google Inc. Cookie Placement

                                   4   Consumer Privacy Litig., 934 F.3d 316, 325 (3d Cir. 2019) (“History and tradition reinforce that a

                                   5   concrete injury for Article III standing purposes occurs when Google, or any other third party,

                                   6   tracks a person’s internet browser activity without authorization.” (emphasis added)).

                                   7          Gabrielli alleges that Motorola “permits the Third Parties to use cookies and

                                   8   other tracking technologies to collect, track, and compile users’ Private Communications,

                                   9   including their browsing history, visit history, website interactions, user input data, demographic

                                  10   information, interests and preferences, shopping behaviors, device information, referring URLs,

                                  11   session information, user identifiers, and/or geolocation data.” Compl. ¶ 91. Contrary to

                                  12   Motorola’s argument that Gabrielli merely described data that Motorola could have collected, the


 United States District Court
                                  13   complaint alleges that Motorola in fact ignored Gabrielli’s rejection of cookies and “continued

                                  14   to cause the placement and/or transmission of cookies along with user data, including those

                                  15   involved in providing personalized content, advertising, analytics, and social media content from



Northern District of California
                                  16   the Third Parties on his device. In doing so, Defendant permitted the Third Parties to track and

                                  17   collect Plaintiff’s Private Communications as Plaintiff browsed the Website.” Id. ¶ 75. Gabrielli’s

                                  18   allegations that Motorola deprived him control of personal information regarding his digital

                                  19   activity and profile are sufficient to establish a concrete injury to his right to privacy and confer

                                  20   standing. See Brown v. Google LLC, 685 F. Supp. 3d 909, 925 (N.D. Cal. 2023) (explaining that

                                  21   under the contextual analysis of determining whether the collection of data gives rise to a concrete

                                  22   injury, Google’s storage of users’ browsing data with unique identifiers to send users personalized

                                  23   ads was sufficient to establish that the plaintiffs suffered concrete harm); see also Mastel v.

                                  24   Miniclip SA, 549 F. Supp. 3d 1129, 1138 (E.D. Cal. 2021).

                                  25          As for Motorola’s “tester” argument, Motorola seeks to apply the cases dismissing

                                  26   invasion of privacy claims brought by tester plaintiffs on the grounds that such persons actually

                                  27   seek out invasions of privacy and so have no reasonable expectation of privacy in their personal

                                  28   information, and thus no injury sufficient to confer standing. See, e.g., Rodriguez v.
                                                                                          10
                                       Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST           Document 40         Filed 07/14/25      Page 11 of 26




                                   1   Autotrader.com, Inc., No. 2:24-CV-08735-RGK-JC, 2025 WL 1122387, at *2 (C.D. Cal. Mar. 14,

                                   2   2025). But these cases do not apply here, given that Gabrielli pleads that he is not a tester and that

                                   3   he visited Motorola’s website to “browse information about Motorola’s products” and not to seek

                                   4   out privacy violations. ECF No. 1 ¶ 70; see also Rodriguez v. Autotrader.com, Inc., No. 2:24-CV-

                                   5   08735-RGK-JC, 2025 WL 1085787 at *1 (C.D. Cal. April 4, 2025) (no dispute that the plaintiff

                                   6   was a tester); id., ECF No. 34 ¶ 4 (plaintiff pleading that she was “a consumer privacy advocate

                                   7   who works as a ‘tester’ to ensure that companies abide by the privacy obligations imposed by

                                   8   California law”). Because the Court considers only the facts alleged in the complaint and accepts

                                   9   those facts as true, there is, as a threshold matter, no basis to consider Gabrielli’s alleged status as

                                  10   a tester in determining his standing.

                                  11          B.      Personal Jurisdiction

                                  12          The parties’ dispute over personal jurisdiction boils down to whether Motorola has


 United States District Court
                                  13   engaged in conduct relating to California sufficient for the Court to exercise specific personal

                                  14   jurisdiction over it in this action. Motorola argues that merely operating a national website

                                  15   accessible in California does not establish purposeful direction toward the forum state.



Northern District of California
                                  16                          a.      Purposeful Direction
                                  17          The first prong of the minimum contact test to determine whether a court may exercise

                                  18   specific personal jurisdiction is often called the “purposeful availment” prong. Yahoo! Inc. v. La

                                  19   Ligue Contre Le Racisme Et L’Antisemitisme, 433 F.3d 1199, 1206 (9th Cir. 2006). The phrase

                                  20   “purposeful availment,” however, is often used “in shorthand fashion, to include both purposeful

                                  21   availment and purposeful direction.” Schwarzenegger, 374 F.3d at 802. “A purposeful availment

                                  22   analysis is most often used in suits sounding in contract.” Id. (citation omitted). “A purposeful

                                  23   direction analysis, on the other hand, is most often used in suits sounding in tort.” Id. (citation

                                  24   omitted). Because Gabrielli’s core claims—CIPA and invasion of privacy—sound in tort, the

                                  25   Court applies a purposeful direction analysis. See Briskin v. Shopify, Inc., 135 F.4th 739, 751 (9th

                                  26   Cir. 2025) (“For claims sounding in tort, like the privacy and data use violations Briskin asserts

                                  27   here, we most often employ a purposeful direction analysis.”).

                                  28          “The purposeful direction test requires that the defendant (1) commit an intentional act,
                                                                                          11
                                       Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST           Document 40        Filed 07/14/25      Page 12 of 26




                                   1   that is (2) expressly aimed at the forum state, and (3) which causes harm that the defendant knows

                                   2   will be suffered in the forum state.” Briskin, 135 F.4th at 751 (internal citation omitted). It is

                                   3   undisputed that Motorola intentionally operates its own website, and thus, Gabrielli’s claims

                                   4   satisfy the first element. See Schwarzenegger, 374 F.3d at 806 (finding that a defendant acts

                                   5   intentionally when he acts with “an intent to perform an actual, physical act in the real world,

                                   6   rather than an intent to accomplish a result or consequence of that act”). With regard to the third

                                   7   element, “[a] defendant causes harm in a particular forum when the ‘bad acts’ that form the basis

                                   8   of the plaintiff’s complaint occur in that forum.” Will Co., Ltd. v. Lee, 47 F.4th 917, 926 (9th Cir.

                                   9   2022), overruled on other grounds by Briskin, 135 F.4th 739. Here, Motorola mainly argues that

                                  10   Gabrielli has not alleged facts showing that it caused any harm to Gabrielli or other individuals in

                                  11   California. See ECF No. 12 at 22. But as the Court explained in its standing analysis, taking

                                  12   Gabrielli’s allegations as true, Motorola was aware that it was collecting the personal information


 United States District Court
                                  13   of California users without their consent. ECF No. 1 ¶¶ 12–13, 75, 91. The third element is

                                  14   therefore satisfied.

                                  15           The remaining element concerns whether Motorola expressly aimed its conduct at the



Northern District of California
                                  16   forum state. “More than two decades ago,” the Ninth Circuit “recognized a distinction between

                                  17   ‘passive’ websites that merely make information available to visitors and ‘interactive’ websites,

                                  18   where ‘users can exchange information with the host computer when the site is interactive.’”

                                  19   Herbal Brands, Inc. v. Photoplaza, Inc., 72 F.4th 1085, 1091 (9th Cir. 2023) (quoting Cybersell,

                                  20   Inc. v. Cybersell, Inc., 130 F.3d 414, 418 (9th Cir. 1997)). Mere passive operation of a website is

                                  21   insufficient to demonstrate express aiming. Briskin, 135 F.4th at 752. Rather, there must be

                                  22   “something more” than just operating the passive website to show express aiming at a forum state.

                                  23   Id.

                                  24           The Ninth Circuit most recently addressed the question of whether operating a passive

                                  25   website that collects personal data from its users constitutes “something more” in Briskin. There,

                                  26   the court found that the plaintiff had made a sufficient showing of jurisdictional facts to establish

                                  27   that Shopify purposely directed its conduct toward California. Id. at 755. It reasoned that

                                  28   Shopify, in its regular course of business, was “alleged to target California consumers to extract,
                                                                                         12
                                       Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST            Document 40        Filed 07/14/25       Page 13 of 26




                                   1   collect, maintain, distribute, and exploit for its own profit, not only the California consumers’

                                   2   payment information that it diverts to its own servers, but also all of the other personal identifying

                                   3   information that it extracts from the software it permanently installs on their devices without their

                                   4   knowledge or consent.” Id. at 755–56. And it found that the effect on the plaintiff was not “mere

                                   5   happenstance” because Shopify “allegedly knew the location of consumers like Briskin either

                                   6   prior to or shortly after installing its initial tracking software onto their devices,” and thus

                                   7   Shopify’s “surreptitious interception of Briskin’s personal identifying information certainly is a

                                   8   relevant contact with the forum state.” Id. at 756, 757.

                                   9           Here, Motorola attempts to distinguish Briskin by arguing that Gabrielli does not allege

                                  10   that he purchased any goods or services from Motorola in California or that Motorola “compiled

                                  11   or sold any data to any merchants, much less including California merchants.” ECF No. 35 at 3–4.

                                  12   The Court does not read the express aiming requirement so narrowly, however, and finds that


 United States District Court
                                  13   Gabrielli has sufficiently alleged jurisdictional facts to establish express aiming.

                                  14           First, Gabrielli argues that Motorola purposefully designed its website to target and collect

                                  15   data from California users, including by claiming compliance with California law in its Privacy



Northern District of California
                                  16   Statement. ECF No. 22 at 14–15. Motorola’s website Privacy Statement specifically identifies

                                  17   California privacy law and claims to comply with it—without specifically mentioning the laws of

                                  18   any other state. See ECF No. 12-3 at 3 (“Motorola does not use or disclose sensitive personal

                                  19   information of consumers for purposes other than those specified at Section 7027(m) of the

                                  20   California Consumer Privacy Act Regulations.”). While the Ninth Circuit in Briskin made clear

                                  21   that “differential targeting” was no longer a requirement for express aiming, it expressly stated

                                  22   that “such treatment may serve as evidence that a defendant expressly aimed its conduct toward

                                  23   the forum state,” as it did in prior cases. Briskin, 135 F.4th at 758 n.17. Motorola’s specific

                                  24   identification of California privacy law in its website Privacy Statement thus serves as evidence of

                                  25   express aiming. See Will Co., 47 F.4th at 925–26 (finding that when a website specifically claims

                                  26   to be legally compliant with a single forum state’s laws, it is appealing to the audience in that

                                  27   forum state and is expressly aiming its site at that forum). Second, as in Briskin, Gabrielli alleges

                                  28   that Motorola knowingly collected the data of California residents because its website allegedly
                                                                                          13
                                       Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST           Document 40          Filed 07/14/25    Page 14 of 26




                                   1   collected IP addresses and geolocation data that revealed where the users resided. ECF No. 31-2

                                   2   at 12 (citing ECF No. 1 ¶¶ 12–13); see Briskin, 135 F.4th at 756.

                                   3          Accordingly, Gabrielli has alleged sufficient jurisdictional facts to show that Motorola

                                   4   purposefully directed its contacts with California.

                                   5                          b.      Arise Out of or Relate to Forum Activities
                                   6          The second prong of the minimum contacts inquiry considers whether the plaintiff’s claims

                                   7   “‘arise out of or relate to the defendant’s contacts with the forum.’” Ford Motor Co. v. Montana

                                   8   Eighth Jud. Dist. Ct., 592 U.S. 351, 359 (2021) (quoting Bristol-Myers Squibb Co., 582 U.S. at

                                   9   262). “The first half of that standard asks about causation; but the back half, after the ‘or,’

                                  10   contemplates that some relationships will support jurisdiction without a causal showing.” Id. at

                                  11   362.

                                  12          Motorola does not directly contest that the second prong of the minimum contacts test is


 United States District Court
                                  13   met here. And the Court finds that the present suit “arise[s] out of or relate[s] to the defendant’s

                                  14   contacts with the forum” because Gabrielli’s claims that his data was collected without his consent

                                  15   relates to Motorola’s connection with the forum—the targeting of California users and collection



Northern District of California
                                  16   of data from California users. See id. at 359.

                                  17                          c.      Fair Play and Substantial Justice
                                  18          Once the plaintiff satisfies the first two prongs, “the burden then shifts to the defendant to

                                  19   ‘present a compelling case’ that the exercise of jurisdiction would not be reasonable.”

                                  20   Schwarzenegger, 374 F.3d at 802 (quoting Burger King Corp., 471 U.S. at 476–78). Motorola

                                  21   does not contest that the exercise of jurisdiction would be reasonable if the other elements are met.

                                  22          Consequently, the Court has personal jurisdiction over Motorola. It now turns to

                                  23   Motorola’s remaining arguments under Rule 12(b)(6).

                                  24          C.      Failure to State a Claim

                                  25                  1.      Privacy Claims

                                  26          Claims for intrusion upon seclusion and invasion of privacy under the California

                                  27   Constitution have “similar elements,” and courts therefore “consider the claims together and ask

                                  28   whether: (1) there exists a reasonable expectation of privacy, and (2) the intrusion was highly
                                                                                         14
                                       Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST           Document 40        Filed 07/14/25      Page 15 of 26




                                   1   offensive.” In re Facebook, Inc. Internet Tracking Litig., 956 F.3d 589, 601 (9th Cir. 2020)

                                   2   (citing Hernandez v. Hillsides, Inc., 47 Cal. 4th 272, 287 (2009)). When evaluating whether a

                                   3   reasonable expectation of privacy exists, “courts consider a variety of factors, including the

                                   4   customs, practices, and circumstances surrounding a defendant’s particular activities.” Id. (citing

                                   5   Hill v. NCAA, 7 Cal. 4th 1, 36 (1994)). The question is “whether a defendant gained ‘unwanted

                                   6   access to data by electronic or other covert means, in violation of the law or social

                                   7   norms.’” Id. (quoting Hernandez, 47 Cal. 4th at 286).

                                   8          Motorola argues that Gabrielli’s invasion of privacy and intrusion upon seclusion claims

                                   9   must be dismissed because (1) Gabrielli has not alleged that Motorola intended to continue using

                                  10   the alleged cookies, as the continued use of cookies was likely due to “improper button

                                  11   functioning;” (2) that the alleged invasions of privacy do not meet the high bar for an egregious

                                  12   breach of social norms; and (3) the complaint alleges only that third-party cookies could track


 United States District Court
                                  13   certain information and not that it did. ECF No. 12 at 23–24. These arguments are unpersuasive

                                  14   and contrary to the facts as alleged in the complaint.

                                  15          First, Motorola’s assertion that the alleged continued usage of cookies was due to a “bona



Northern District of California
                                  16   fide error,” ECF No. 12 at 23, “is an inherently factual contention that cannot properly be resolved

                                  17   on a motion to dismiss.” Brennan v. Concord EFS, Inc., 369 F. Supp. 2d 1127, 1131 (N.D. Cal.

                                  18   2005). And the Court rejects Motorola’s argument that “common sense” dictates that the button

                                  19   rejecting all cookies must have failed to function properly, ECF No. 27 at 13—as opposed to a

                                  20   plain reading of the complaint suggesting that Motorola intentionally disregarded Gabrielli’s

                                  21   having opted out and continued to use cookies on his device. See ECF No. 1 ¶ 2 (“Defendant’s

                                  22   promises [of allowing users to reject all non-essential cookies] are outright lies, designed to lull

                                  23   users into a false sense of security.”). Because Motorola’s argument is based on a dispute of facts,

                                  24   the Court declines to dismiss Gabrielli’s claims on this basis, as it must “accept all factual

                                  25   allegations in the complaint as true and construe the pleadings in the light most favorable to the

                                  26   nonmoving party.” Knievel, 393 F.3d at 1072.

                                  27          Second, it is premature to dismiss Gabrielli’s claims based on a determination of how

                                  28   offensive or serious the privacy intrusion is. “Under California law, courts must be reluctant to
                                                                                         15
                                       Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST           Document 40        Filed 07/14/25      Page 16 of 26




                                   1   reach a conclusion at the pleading stage about how offensive or serious the privacy intrusion is.”

                                   2   In re Facebook, Inc., Consumer Priv. User Profile Litig., 402 F. Supp. 3d 767, 797 (N.D. Cal.

                                   3   2019). When determining whether an invasion is “highly offensive,” courts consider “the degree

                                   4   and setting of the intrusion,” as well as “the intruder’s motives and objectives.” Hernandez v.

                                   5   Hillsides, Inc., 47 Cal. 4th 272, 287 (Cal. 2009). Given the factually intensive nature of the

                                   6   inquiry, “[c]ourts are generally hesitant to decide claims of this nature at the pleading stage.” In re

                                   7   Meta Pixel Healthcare Litig., 647 F. Supp. 3d at 799. Only if the allegations “show no reasonable

                                   8   expectation of privacy or an insubstantial impact on privacy interests” can the “question of [a

                                   9   serious or highly offensive] invasion [ ] be adjudicated as a matter of law.” Hill, 7 Cal. 4th at 40.

                                  10   Gabrielli has alleged enough in the complaint such that the Court cannot, at least at this stage of

                                  11   the proceedings, conclude as a matter of law that the alleged tracking of Gabrielli’s IP address,

                                  12   browsing history, and user preferences is not highly offensive. See Katz-Lacabe v. Oracle Am.,


 United States District Court
                                  13   Inc., 668 F. Supp. 3d 928, 942–43 (N.D. Cal. 2023).

                                  14          Third, contrary to Motorola’s contentions that Gabrielli only alleges what Motorola’s

                                  15   cookies could track, the complaint specifically alleges that Motorola “caus[ed] third party cookies



Northern District of California
                                  16   to be stored on consumers’ devices and browsers that enabled the Third Parties to track and collect

                                  17   Plaintiff’s and Class member’s Private Communications, including their browsing history, visit

                                  18   history, website interactions, user input data, demographic information, interests and preferences,

                                  19   shopping behaviors, device information, referring URLs, session information, user identifiers,

                                  20   and/or geolocation data . . . .” ECF No. 1 ¶ 164; see also id. ¶ 27. The Court therefore rejects

                                  21   Motorola’s argument on this basis and accordingly denies Motorola’s motion to dismiss

                                  22   Gabrielli’s invasion of privacy claims.

                                   1   because the section applies only to telephones, and Gabrielli has not alleged that the device he

                                   9   Penal Code § 638.50(b). If the drafters of Section 638.50 had intended for “pen register” to be

                                  10   limited to telephone technologies, they knew how to do so—as evidenced by other sections of

                                  11   CIPA where they imposed such limitations. See, e.g., Cal. Penal Code §§ 631(a), 632.7. For these

                                  12   reasons, other federal courts have interpreted the plain language of Section 638.50 expansively to


 United States District Court
                                  13   find that “pen registers” includes data collection tools beyond those the record information from

                                  14   telephones. As the court in Greenley v. Kochava, Inc., explained:

                                  19                  4.      Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

                                  20          “Every contract imposes on each party a duty of good faith and fair dealing in each

                                  21   performance and in its enforcement.” Careau & Co. v. Sec. Pac. Bus. Credit, Inc., 222 Cal. App.

                                  22   3d 1371, 1393 (1990) (citations and internal quotation marks omitted). “Or, to put it another way,

                                  23   the implied covenant imposes upon each party the obligation to do everything that the contract

                                  24   presupposes they will do to accomplish its purpose.” Id. (citation and internal quotation marks

                                  25   omitted). A “breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing involves something

                                  26   beyond breach of the contractual duty itself and it has been held that bad faith implies unfair

                                  27   dealing rather than mistaken judgment.” Id. at 1394 (citation and internal quotation marks

                                  28   omitted) (cleaned up). Thus, to state a claim for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and
                                                                                        20
                                       Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST            Document 40         Filed 07/14/25    Page 21 of 26




                                   1   fair dealing, the plaintiff must allege facts that

                                   2                   show that the conduct of the defendant, whether or not it also
                                                       constitutes a breach of a consensual contract term, demonstrates a
                                   3                   failure or refusal to discharge contractual responsibilities, prompted
                                                       not by an honest mistake, bad judgment or negligence but rather by
                                   4                   a conscious and deliberate act, which unfairly frustrates the agreed
                                                       common purposes and disappoints the reasonable expectations of
                                   5                   the other party thereby depriving that party of the benefits of the
                                                       agreement. Just what conduct will meet this criteria must be
                                   6                   determined on a case by case basis and will depend on the
                                                       contractual purposes and reasonably justified expectations of the
                                   7                   parties.
                                   8                   If the allegations do not go beyond the statement of a mere contract
                                                       breach and, relying on the same alleged acts, simply seek the same
                                   9                   damages or other relief already claimed in a companion contract
                                                       cause of action, they may be disregarded as superfluous as no
                                  10                   additional claim is actually stated.
                                  11   Id. at 1395; see also Bionghi v. Metro. Water Dist. of S. Cal., 70 Cal. App. 4th 1358, 1370 (1999)

                                  12   (holding that, where the “claim of breach of the implied covenant relies on the same acts, and


 United States District Court
                                  13   seeks the same damages, as [the plaintiff’s] claim for breach of contract,” the court may dismiss

                                  14   the claim for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing as duplicative).

                                  15           Motorola argues that Gabrielli’s claim for breach of the implied covenant “mimics his



Northern District of California
                                  16   breach of contract claim, and so should be dismissed as superfluous.” ECF No. 12 at 32.

                                  17   Gabrielli does not respond to this argument in his opposition and thus concedes the point. See

                                  18   Tyler v. Travelers Com. Ins. Co., 499 F. Supp. 3d 693, 701 (N.D. Cal. 2020) (“Plaintiff concedes

                                  19   these arguments by failing to address them in her opposition.” (collecting cases)). The Court finds

                                  20   that Gabrielli’s claim for breach of the implied covenant suffers from the same defective damages

                                  21   allegations as does his claim for breach of contract.

                                  22           Accordingly, the Court dismisses Gabrielli’s claim for breach of the implied covenant of

                                  24                   5.      Fraud and/or Misrepresentation

                                  25           “Averments of fraud must be accompanied by the who, what, when, where, and how of the

                                  26   misconduct charged.” Kearns v. Ford Motor Co., 567 F.3d 1120, 1124 (9th Cir. 2009) (internal

                                  27   citation and quotation omitted). Motorola argues that Gabrielli fails to meet these requirements.

                                  28           The Court disagrees. The complaint states that the “who” is Motorola; the “what” are
                                                                                            21
                                       Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST           Document 40        Filed 07/14/25      Page 22 of 26




                                   1   misrepresentations by Motorola through the popup banner offering users the option to “Reject All

                                   2   non-essential cookies;” the “when” is the multiple occasions that Gabrielli visited Motorola’s

                                   3   website between February 2022 and January 2024; the “where” is on Motorola’s website; and the

                                   4   “how” is through the storage of cookies on users’ devices that transmit personal information about

                                   5   the users. See ECF No. 1 ¶¶ 2, 22–24, 27, 44, 50 70–76. These allegations satisfy the “who, what,

                                   6   when, and where” of Rule 9(b). See, e.g., Amin v. Subway Restaurants, Inc., No. 21-CV-00498-

                                   7   JST, 2022 WL 20184652, at *4 (N.D. Cal. July 7, 2022) (finding Rule 9(b) satisfied where “the

                                   8   ‘who’ is Subway; the ‘what’ are misrepresentations and/or omissions by Subway through

                                   9   marketing, labeling, and advertising its tuna salad products as ‘tuna;’ the ‘when’ is since 2013 and

                                  10   throughout the Class Period; and the ‘where’ is Subway's in-store and online menus”); Clancy v.

                                  11   The Bromley Tea Co., 308 F.R.D. 564, 576 (N.D. Cal. 2013) (finding Rule 9(b) satisfied where

                                  12   “the ‘who’ is Bromley Tea Company and other defendants; the ‘what’ is nine discrete types of


 United States District Court
                                  13   unlawful and deceptive claims by Defendants on the labeling and packaging of its products,

                                  14   including Pure Green Tea and 100% Organic Pure Black Tea, as well as on its website; the ‘when’

                                  15   is since 2008 and throughout the class period; the ‘where’ is Bromley’s package labels and



Northern District of California
                                  16   website”); Robinson v. J.M. Smucker Co., No. 18-cv-04654-HSG, 2019 WL 2029069, at *3 (N.D.

                                  17   Cal. May 8, 2019) (finding the plaintiff satisfied Rule 9(b) by alleging “that she purchased Crisco

                                  18   EVOO from a Wal-Mart in Antioch multiple times in 2017 and 2018 in reliance on the

                                  19   packaging’s purportedly false claim that it was entirely extra virgin olive oil”).

                                  20          Motorola also rehashes its “bona fide error” argument to contend that Gabrielli cannot

                                  21   allege that Motorola knew any statements it made were untrue. For the reasons explained above,

                                  22   the Court rejects that argument because it is based in facts not alleged in the complaint. Gabrielli

                                  23   has specifically alleged that Motorola did know that its representations regarding the “Reject All”

                                  24   button were false and that it intentionally misrepresented its cookies policy to collect users’ data.

                                  25   See, e.g., ECF No. 1 ¶¶ 2, 3, 14, 116.

                                  26          The Court thus denies Motorola’s motion to dismiss Gabrielli’s fraud and/or

                                  27   misrepresentation claim.

                                   1                   6.     Trespass to Chattels

                                   2           In California, trespass to chattels “lies where an intentional interference with the

                                   3   possession of personal property has proximately caused injury.” In re Apple Inc. Device

                                   4   Performance Litig., 347 F. Supp. 3d 434, 455 (N.D. Cal. 2018) (quoting Thrifty-Tel, Inc. v.

                                   5   Bezenek, 46 Cal. App. 4th 1559, 54 Cal. Rptr. 2d 468, 473 (1996)). The California Supreme Court

                                   6   has held that the principles underlying the tort apply to allegations of digital trespass. See

                                   7   Intel Corp. v. Hamidi, 30 Cal. 4th 1342 (2003). Thus, “[i]n order to prevail on a claim for trespass

                                   8   based on accessing a computer system, the plaintiff must establish: (1) defendant intentionally and

                                   9   without authorization interfered with plaintiff’s possessory interest in the computer system; and

                                  10   (2) defendant's unauthorized use proximately resulted in damage.” eBay, Inc. v. Bidder’s Edge,

                                  11   Inc., 100 F. Supp. 2d 1058, 1069–70 (N.D. Cal. 2000) (citing Thrifty-Tel, Inc., 54 Cal. Rptr. 2d at

                                  12   473). A plaintiff may satisfy the damages element by pleading that the trespass “impaired the


 United States District Court
                                  13   condition, quality, or value of the personal property.” Fields v. Wise Media, LLC, No. 12-CV-

                                  14   05160 WHA, 2013 WL 5340490, at *4 (N.D. Cal. Sept. 24, 2013) (citing Hamidi, 30 Cal.

                                  15   4th at 1356).



Northern District of California
                                  16           Motorola challenges only the second element of damages—contending that Gabrielli has

                                  17   not adequately alleged any damage to or impaired functioning of his device. The Court agrees.

                                  18   Gabrielli’s alleges that Motorola’s trespass “caused Plaintiff and other users the following

                                  19   damages: Nominal damages for trespass; reduction of storage, disk space, and performance[]; and

                                  20   loss of value of Plaintiff’s and other users’ computing devices.” ECF No. 1 ¶ 184. These

                                  21   allegations are too conclusory to state a claim for trespass to chattels, as Gabrielli “offer[s] no

                                  22   factual support for these allegations. And it is not obvious how the presence on one’s computer of

                                  23   the cookies from the providers’ websites would result in any cognizable reduction in storage, disk

                                  24   space, or performance.” Doe I v. Google LLC, 741 F. Supp. 3d 828, 846 (N.D. Cal. 2024)

                                  25   (internal citation omitted). Accordingly, the Court dismisses Gabrielli’s claim for trespass to

                                  26   chattels.

                                  27                   7.     Unjust Enrichment

                                  28           Gabrielli asserts a claim for unjust enrichment based on Motorola’s “scheme to increase its
                                                                                         23
                                       Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST            Document 40        Filed 07/14/25       Page 24 of 26




                                   1   own profits through a pervasive pattern of false statements and fraudulent omissions” relating to

                                   2   the “Reject All” button and usage of cookies. ECF No. 1 ¶¶ 152–53. Motorola argues that the

                                   3   Court should disallow this claim because Gabrielli has separately brought a breach of contract

                                   4   claim and thus alleges the existence of an enforceable, binding agreement covering the same

                                   5   rights. ECF No. 12 at 35–36.

                                   6           “[A]s a matter of law, a quasi-contract action for unjust enrichment does not lie where . . .

                                   7   express binding agreements exist and define the parties’ rights.” California Med. Ass’n, Inc. v.

                                   8   Aetna U.S. Healthcare of California, Inc., 94 Cal. App. 4th 151, 172, 114 Cal. Rptr. 2d 109, 125

                                   9   (2001). Nonetheless, “[a] plaintiff may assert inconsistent theories of recovery at the pleading

                                  10   stage, including inconsistent claims alleging both the existence and the absence of an enforceable

                                  11   contract.” Saroya v. Univ. of the Pac., 503 F.Supp.3d 986, 998 (N.D. Cal. 2020) (citing Fed. R.

                                  12   Civ. P. 8). “However, a plaintiff may not plead the existence of an enforceable contract and


 United States District Court
                                  13   simultaneously maintain a quasi-contract claim unless the plaintiff also pleads facts suggesting

                                  14   that the contract may be unenforceable or invalid.” Id. at 998 (citing Klein v. Chevron U.S.A., Inc.,

                                  15   202 Cal. App. 4th 1342, 1389 (2023)). Given that the Court has dismissed Gabrielli’s breach of



Northern District of California
                                  16   contract claim, it declines to dismiss his claim for unjust enrichment on this basis.

                                  17           D.      Motion to Strike

                                  18           Motorola asks the Court to strike the class allegations under either Rule 12(f) or Rule 23.

                                  19   Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(f) authorizes the Court to “strike from a pleading . . . any

                                  20   redundant, immaterial, impertinent, or scandalous matter.” Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(f). “The function of

                                  21   a 12(f) motion to strike is to avoid the expenditure of time and money that must arise from

                                  22   litigating spurious issues by dispensing with those issues prior to trial . . . .” Whittlestone, Inc. v.

                                  23   Handi-Craft Co., 618 F.3d 970, 973 (9th Cir. 2010) (quotation marks and citations omitted).

                                  24   Similarly, Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(d)(1)(D) allows the Court to “issue orders

                                  25   that . . . require that the pleadings be amended to eliminate allegations about representation of

                                  26   absent persons and that the action proceed accordingly.” Fed. R. Civ. P. 23(d)(1)(D).

                                  27           “[A] motion to strike class claims based only on the pleadings is proper only if the court is

                                  28   ‘convinced that any questions of law are clear and not in dispute, and that under no set of
                                                                                          24
                                       Case 4:24-cv-09533-JST           Document 40        Filed 07/14/25      Page 25 of 26




                                   1   circumstances could the claim or defense succeed.’” Parducci v. Overland Sols., Inc., 399 F.

                                   2   Supp. 3d 969, 976 (N.D. Cal. 2019) (quoting In re iPad Unlimited Data Plan Litig., 2012 WL

                                   3   2428248, at *2 (N.D. Cal. June 26, 2012)). “[D]ismissal of class allegations at the pleading stage

                                   4   should be done rarely and . . . the better course is to deny such a motion because the shape and

                                   5   form of a class action evolves only through the process of discovery.” In re Wal-Mart Stores, Inc.

                                   6   Wage & Hour Litig., 505 F. Supp. 2d 609, 615 (N.D. Cal. 2007) (quoting Myers v. MedQuist, Inc.,

                                   7   No. 05-4608, 2006 WL 3751210, *4 (D.N.J. 2006) (internal quotation omitted)); see also 7AA

                                   8   Charles Alan Wright, Arthur R. Miller & Mary K. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure Civil

                                   9   § 1785.3 (3d. 2005) (providing that the practice employed in the overwhelming majority of class

                                  10   actions is to address class certification issues only after an appropriate period of discovery).

                                  11          Here, Motorola argues that the putative class cannot be certified because (1) Gabrielli lacks

                                  12   Article III standing; (2) Gabrielli and the putative class members agreed to bring their claims on an


 United States District Court
                                  13   individual basis under the Terms of Use; (3) the putative class cannot meet the requirements of

                                  14   Rule 23(a) and (b); and (4) the putative class definition includes members who would have claims

                                  15   that are time barred under certain of the asserted causes of action. See generally ECF No. 13.



Northern District of California
                                  16   Gabrielli argues that Motorola’s motion is improper because it attempts to make additional

                                  17   12(b)(1) arguments rather than arguing that Gabrielli’s class claims advance ‘“spurious issues.’”

                                  18   ECF No. 21 at 6 (quoting Whittlestone, 618 F.3d at 973).

                                  19          This is not an exceptional case that warrants the class claims being stricken at this stage.

                                  20   As this Court has done in prior cases, it “declines to strike the class allegations here where

                                  21   discovery has not yet commenced and the issues raised by [Defendants] are ones to be addressed

                                  22   at the class certification, not the motion to dismiss, stage.” Gatling-Lee v. Del Monte Foods, Inc.,

                                  23   No. 22-CV-00892-JST, 2023 WL 11113888, at *14 (N.D. Cal. Mar. 28, 2023), reconsideration

                                  24   denied sub nom. Nacarino v. Del Monte Foods, Inc., No. 22-CV-00892-JST, 2024 WL 847925

                                  25   (N.D. Cal. Feb. 28, 2024); see also F.G. v. CooperSurgical, Inc., No. 24-CV-01261-JST, 2024

                                  26   WL 4982991, at *9 (N.D. Cal. Dec. 4, 2024); Williams v. Affinity Ins. Servs., Inc., No. 23-CV-

                                  27   06347-JST, 2024 WL 3153214, at *8 (N.D. Cal. June 24, 2024); Bui-Ford v. Tesla, Inc., No. 4:23-

                                  28   CV-02321, 2024 WL 694485, at *8 (N.D. Cal. Feb. 20, 2024); Dodson v. Tempur-Sealy Int’l, Inc.,

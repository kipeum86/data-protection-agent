11                                        II.    LEGAL STANDARD
12          Under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6), a party may file a motion to dismiss
13   on the grounds that a complaint “fail[s] to state a claim upon which relief can be granted.”
14   Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(6). A motion to dismiss under Rule 12(b)(6) “tests the legal
15   sufficiency of a claim.” Navarro v. Block, 250 F.3d 729, 732 (9th Cir. 2001). To survive
16   a motion to dismiss, “a complaint must contain sufficient factual matter, accepted as true,
17   to ‘state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face.’” Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662,
18   678 (2009) (quoting Bell Atl. Corp. v. Twombly, 550 U.S. 544, 570 (2007)). “A claim has
19   facial plausibility when the plaintiff pleads factual content that allows the court to draw the
20   reasonable inference that the defendant is liable for the misconduct alleged.”                            Id.
21   “Determining whether a complaint states a plausible claim for relief will . . . be a context-
22   specific task that requires the reviewing court to draw on its judicial experience and
23   common sense.” Id. at 679. “Factual allegations must be enough to raise a right to relief
24   above the speculative level.” Twombly, 550 U.S. at 555. If Plaintiff “ha[s] not nudged”

 3         In reviewing the plausibility of a complaint on a motion to dismiss, a court must

 9   Sprewell v. Golden State Warriors, 266 F.3d 979, 988 (9th Cir. 2001)).
10         When a court grants a motion to dismiss a complaint, it must then decide whether to
11   grant leave to amend. Leave to amend should be “freely given” where there is no (1)
12   “undue delay,” (2) “bad faith or dilatory motive,” (3) “undue prejudice to the opposing
13   party” if amendment were allowed, or (4) “futility” in allowing amendment. Foman v.
14   Davis, 371 U.S. 178, 182 (1962). Dismissal without leave to amend is proper only if it is
15   clear that “the complaint could not be saved by any amendment.” Intri-Plex Techs. v. Crest
16   Group, Inc., 499 F.3d 1048, 1056 (9th Cir. 2007). “Leave need not be granted where the
17   amendment of the complaint . . . constitutes an exercise in futility . . . .” Ascon Props., Inc.
18   v. Mobil Oil Co., 866 F.2d 1149, 1160 (9th Cir. 1989).
19                                       III.   DISCUSSION
20         Plaintiff again fails to state a claim because she fails to plausibly allege the existence
21   of a third-party eavesdropper or that Plaintiff aided and abetted any such eavesdropper.
22   Section 631(a) makes actionable “three distinct and mutually independent patterns of
23   conduct: intentional wiretapping, willfully attempting to learn the contents or meaning of
24   a communication in transit over a wire, and attempting to use or communicate information
25   obtained as a result of engaging in either of the previous two activities.” Tavernetti v.
26   Super. Ct. of San Diego Cnty., 22 Cal. 3d 187, 192 (1978). Section 631 also imposes
27   liability upon “anyone ‘who aids, agrees with, employs, or conspires with any person or
28   persons to unlawfully do, or permit, or cause to be done any of the’ . . . three bases for

16   under Clause Four. “To state a claim under [Clause Four], Plaintiff must allege [the third-
17   party service] has violated one of the first three clauses of section 631(a), and that
18   Defendant aided, agreed with, employed, or conspired with that person or entity to commit
19   those unlawful acts.” Esparza v. UAG Escondido A1 Inc., No. 23-cv-102 DMS, 2024 WL
20   559241, at *2 (S.D. Cal. Feb. 12, 2024). Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s attempt to
21   replead a Clause Four claim fails for two reasons: (1) the FAC fails to plausibly allege the
22   existence of a third-party eavesdropper because it only added more speculative and
23   conclusory allegations; and (2) even if the FAC alleged a third-party “eavesdropper,” it
24   failed to plausibly allege a predicate violation that Defendant could have aided and abetted.
25   (Def.’s Mot. at 10–11, 15.) Plaintiff responds that it has plausibly alleged the existence of
26   a third-party eavesdropper as well as a predicate violation. (Pl.’s Opp’n at 2.) The Court
27   addresses these arguments below.

12         1. Section 631(a), Clause One
13         As noted, Plaintiff alleges the underlying third-party wrongdoer in this case is
14   Salesforce. To show that Salesforce violated Section 631(a), Clause One, Plaintiff must
15   allege Salesforce intentionally tapped or made an unauthorized connection “with any
16   telegraph or telephone wire, line, cable, or instrument, including the wire, line, cable, or
17   instrument of any internal telephonic communication system[.]” Cal. Penal Code § 631(a).
18   Defendant argues the use of a chat feature on a website does not meet the “telegraph or
19   telephone” element of this claim, and therefore, Plaintiff cannot show a violation of Clause
20   One. (Def.’s Mot. at 16.) Plaintiff disagrees and asserts that Clause One applies to
21   communications via smartphone. (Pl.’s Opp’n at 16–18.)
22         In Valenzuela v. Keurig Green Mountain, Inc., --- F. Supp. 3d ----, No. 22-CV-9042-
23   JSC, 2023 WL 3707181, at *2 (N.D. Cal. May 24, 2023), a case based on similar facts and
24   filed by Plaintiff’s counsel in this case, the court addressed the same arguments raised here,
25   and concluded, based on the plain language of the statute, that the statute did not apply “to
26   technologies other than telegraphs and telephones.” This Court has followed Valenzuela’s
27   reasoning in earlier cases and does so again here. See UAG Escondido, 2024 WL 559241,
28   at *2 (finding Valenzuela’s reasoning persuasive and adopting its reasoning). The Court

11   Two because (a) Plaintiff consented to the recording of her chat messages with Defendant,
12   (b) any alleged use of a recording software fell within the “party exception” and was not
13   actionable, and (c) Plaintiff has failed to allege facts to support the “in transit” requirement
14   of Clause Two. (Def.’s Mot. at 16–24.) Plaintiff responds that (a) she did not consent to
15   Salesforce recording her conversation with Defendant, (b) the alleged use of third-party

18   from California, which also satisfies this element.
19         CIPA contains “an exemption from liability for a person who is a ‘party’ to the
20   communication” because such parties cannot eavesdrop on their own conversation. In re
21   Facebook, Inc. Internet Tracking Litig., 956 F.3d 589, 607 (9th Cir. 2020); see Rogers v.
22   Ulrich, 52 Cal. App. 3d 894, 899 (1975) (“It is never a secret to one party to a conversation
23   that the other party is listening . . . .”). Courts in California “are split on whether this
24   exemption extends to third parties, particularly, third party software providers.” UAG
25   Escondido, 2024 WL 559241, at *4. “The first set of cases holds that software providers .
26   . . are third parties within the meaning of Section 631 and Ribas.” Javier v. Assurance IQ,
27   LLC, 649 F. Supp. 3d 891, 899 (N.D. Cal. 2023) (citing Ribas v. Clark, 38 Cal. 3d 355,
28   358, 360–61 (1985) (wife’s friend listening in on wife’s conversation with her former

 4   demonstrating that Salesforce “use[d], or attempt[ed] to use, in any manner, or for any
 5   purpose, or to communicate in any way, any information” obtained as the result of a
 6   violation of the preceding clauses. Cal. Penal Code § 631. A violation of Clause Three is
 7   contingent upon a violation of Clauses One or Two. See In re Google Assistant Privacy
 8   Litig., 457 F. Supp. 3d 797, 827 (N.D. Cal. 2020) (“Plaintiffs must establish that the
 9   information at issue . . . was obtained through a violation of the first or second clauses [of
10   § 631(a)]. Because Plaintiffs have not done so, they have also failed to plead a violation
11   of the third clause.”) Here, Plaintiff has failed to allege a predicate violation of Clauses
12   One or Two, so she necessarily fails to allege a predicate violation of Clause Three.
13   Accordingly, because Plaintiff has failed to allege a predicate violation under Clauses One,
14   Two, or Three, Plaintiff has failed to plausibly allege an aiding-abetting claim against
15   Defendant under Clause Four.
16                                     IV.    CONCLUSION
17         For the foregoing reasons, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to dismiss.
18   Because it is not clear that amendment would be futile, the complaint is dismissed with
19   leave to amend. Within fourteen (14) days of the date of this Order, Plaintiff may file a
20   second amended complaint which cures the pleading deficiencies identified in this Order.
21   Plaintiff is cautioned that if the second amended complaint fails to cure the identified
22   deficiencies, the Court will dismiss the complaint without further leave to amend.

19          Plaintiff Adrian Scott Randles brings this putative class action against Defendants Nationstar
20   Mortgage, LLC, dba Mr. Cooper, and Mr. Cooper Group, Inc. (collectively, “Defendants” or “Mr.
21   Cooper”), alleging a violation of the California Consumer Privacy Act in connection with a data breach
22   resulting from a cyber attack on Defendants. Doc. 1 (complaint). Defendants move pursuant to 28
23   U.S.C. § 1404(a) for a transfer of venue to the Northern District of Texas, Dallas Division, where
24   multiple other putative class actions concerning the same data breach are currently pending. Doc. 15.
25   In the alternative, Defendants request transfer under the first-to-file rule. Id. Plaintiff filed an
26   opposition to the motion, to which Defendants replied. Docs. 18, 24. The Court heard argument on the
27   motion on April 17, 2024. For the reasons set forth below, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion to
28   transfer venue.

 1   I.       Background

 2            Plaintiff is a resident of Los Banos, California, and held a mortgage loan serviced by

 3   Defendants. Doc. 1 at 7. Defendants are incorporated in Delaware and have their principal place of

 4   business in Coppell, Texas, which is in the Northern District of Texas, Dallas Division. Id. at 6; Doc.

 5   15-2 at 2. The majority of Defendants’ employees, including the cyber security team, are based in

 6   Texas. Doc. 15-2.

 8   addresses, bank account information, and Social Security Numbers of Plaintiff and others in

 9   connection with servicing their mortgage loans. Doc. 1 at 7. On or about December 16, 2023,

13   failure to maintain reasonable security procedures and practices to protect consumers’ personal

14   information, in violation of Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.150, the California Consumer Privacy Act

15   (“CCPA”). Doc. 1 at 7-8, 10-11, 14-15. Plaintiff filed this action on February 7, 2024. Doc. 1. The

27   filing of this action, approximately twenty-five additional putative nationwide class actions were filed

 3   II.     Legal Standard

 4           A.      Motion to transfer venue

 5           Pursuant to 28 U.S.C. § 1404, “[f]or the convenience of parties and witnesses, in the interest of

 6   justice, a district court may transfer any civil action to any other district or division where it might

 7   have been brought.” 28 U.S.C. § 1404(a). The initial inquiry under § 1404(a) is whether the case

 8   could have been brought in the forum to which transfer is sought. Id.; Hatch v. Reliance Ins. Co., 758

 9   F.2d 409, 414 (9th Cir. 1985). If venue is appropriate in the proposed transferee court, the statute

10   provides “discretion in the district court to adjudicate motions for transfer according to an

11   ‘individualized, case-by-case consideration of convenience and fairness.’” Stewart Org., Inc. v. Ricoh

12   Corp., 487 U.S. 22, 29 (1988) (quoting Van Dusen v. Barrack, 376 U.S. 612, 622 (1964)).

13           Among the non-exclusive factors a district court may consider in determining whether a

14   transfer is warranted for the convenience of the parties and witnesses and in the interest of justice are:

15   (1) the plaintiff's choice of forum, (2) the respective parties' contacts with the forum, (3) contacts

16   relating to the plaintiff's cause of action in the chosen forum, (4) differences in the costs of litigation in

17   the two forums, (5) the availability of compulsory process to compel attendance of unwilling non-

18   party witnesses, (6) ease of access to sources of proof, (7) the location where relevant agreements, if

19   any, were negotiated and executed, and (8) the state that is most familiar with the governing law.

20   Jones v. GNC Franchising, Inc., 211 F.3d 495, 498-99 (9th Cir. 2000). Courts also consider the

21   feasibility of consolidation with a pending related action in the forum to which transfer has been

22   proposed. A.J. Indus., Inc. v. U.S. Dist. Court for Cent. Dist. of Cal., 503 F.2d 384, 389 (9th Cir.

23   1974); Hawkins v. Gerber Prods. Co., 924 F. Supp. 2d 1208, 1214 (S.D. Cal. 2013) (transfer

24   supported where five similar cases against defendants were already consolidated and pending in other

25   district). The party seeking a change of venue has the burden of demonstrating transfer is appropriate.

26   Commodity Futures Trading Comm’n v. Savage, 611 F.2d 270, 279 (9th Cir. 1979).

 1             B.     First-to-file rule

 2             Under the first-to-file rule, “when cases involving the same parties and issues have been filed

 3   in two different districts, the second district court has discretion to transfer, stay, or dismiss the second

 4   case in the interest of efficiency and judicial economy.” Cedars-Sinai Med. Ctr. v. Shalala, 125 F.3d

 5   765, 769 (9th Cir. 1997); Alltrade, Inc. v. Uniweld Prods., Inc., 946 F.2d 622, 628 (9th Cir. 1991).

 6   The rule is a “‘generally recognized doctrine of federal comity’ permitting a district court to decline

 7   jurisdiction over an action.” Wallerstein v. Dole Fresh Vegetables, Inc., 967 F. Supp. 2d 1289, 1292

 8   (N.D. Cal. 2013) (quoting Inherent.com v. Martindale-Hubbell, 420 F. Supp. 2d 1093, 1097 (N.D. Cal.

 9   2006)).

10   III.      Discussion and Analysis

11             A.     Motion to transfer venue

12             This action could have been brought in the Northern District of Texas. Under the Class Action

13   Fairness Act, minimal diversity is satisfied as Plaintiff is a resident of California, Defendants are

14   Delaware corporations with their principal place of business in Coppell, Texas, and the aggregated

15   claims exceed $5 million. See 28 U.S.C. § 1332(d). The Northern District of Texas has personal

16   jurisdiction over Defendants as their principal place of business is located there. Venue is proper in

17   that district as the Defendants reside there and a substantial portion of the events or omissions giving

18   rise to Plaintiff’s claim occurred there. 28 U.S.C. § 1391; see Doc. 1. Plaintiff does not dispute that

19   this action could have been brought in the Northern District of Texas. See Doc. 18.

20             The Court therefore turns to whether transfer is warranted for the convenience of the parties

21   and witnesses and in the interests of justice.

22                    1.      Feasibility of consolidation with other actions

23             Defendants argue that the pendency of 26 substantially similar actions in the Northern District

24   of Texas, consolidated under the Cabezas action, weighs in favor of transfer. Doc. 15-1 at 16. The

25   Court agrees. Transfer to a court already trying similar claims based on the same incident would serve

26   the convenience of the parties and witnesses, permit the potential consolidation of discovery, promote

27   judicial economy, and avoid inconsistent and conflicting resolutions. “The feasibility of consolidation

28   is a significant factor in a transfer decision, although even the pendency of an action in another district

                                                           4
         Case 1:24-cv-00177-KES-SKO Document 31 Filed 04/26/24 Page 5 of 12


 1   is important because of the positive effects it might have in possible consolidation of discovery and

 2   convenience to witnesses and parties.” A.J. Indus., 503 F.2d 384 at 386–87 (internal citations

 3   omitted); Hawkins, 924 F. Supp. 2d at 1214. “To permit a situation in which two cases involving

 4   precisely the same issues are simultaneously pending in different District Courts leads to the

 5   wastefulness of time, energy and money that § 1404(a) was designed to prevent.” Cont’l Grain Co. v.

 6   Barge FBL-585, 364 U.S. 19, 26 (1960).

 7           The Cabezas action was filed before Plaintiff filed suit, the putative nationwide class in the

 8   consolidated Cabezas action includes the sub-class of California plaintiffs at issue in this case, and

 9   numerous other cases arising from the same data breach have already been consolidated in the

10   Northern District of Texas under the Cabezas action. Plaintiff argues the Cabezas action is not

11   substantially related because it does not include a CCPA claim. Doc. 18 at 16. However, while the

12   Cabezas action does not include a claim under the CCPA, the negligence claims and other claims in

13   Cabezas raise similar factual and legal issues as the CCPA claim on the core question regarding

14   whether Defendants breached their duty to implement and maintain appropriate security with respect

15   to the plaintiffs’ personal information.1 The actions involve substantially similar evidence and

16   elements of proof. Transfer will also minimize the risk of duplicative litigation and inconsistent

17   rulings concerning Defendant’s conduct regarding the data security incident. This factor accordingly

18   weighs in favor of a transfer of venue.

19                  2.      Plaintiff’s choice of forum

20           Ordinarily courts attach a “strong presumption in favor of the plaintiff’s choice of forum.”

21   Piper Aircraft v. Reyno 454 U.S. 235, 255 (1981); Decker Coal v. Commonwealth Edison Co., 805

22   F.2d 834, 843 (9th Cir. 1986). However, in class actions a plaintiff’s choice of forum is often

23   accorded less weight. Lou v. Belzberg, 834 F.2d 730, 739 (9th Cir. 1987). In evaluating the weight to

24

26   nature of the information to protect the personal information.” Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.150(a)(1).
     Similarly, the negligence claim in the Cabezas action asserts that Mr. Cooper had a duty to exercise

     breached that duty by failing to employ industry standard data and cybersecurity measures, and that its
28   breach resulted in the unauthorized third-party access to the class members’ personal information.

 1   be accorded to the plaintiff’s choice of forum in a class action a court must also consider the extent of

 2   both parties’ contacts with the forum, including those contacts relating to the plaintiff’s cause of

 3   action. Id. A plaintiff’s choice “is entitled to only minimal consideration” if the Court finds “the

 4   operative facts have not occurred within the forum and the forum has no interest in the parties or

 5   subject matter.” Id.

 6          Plaintiff’s cause of action concerns Defendants’ alleged failure “to institute reasonable security

 7   procedures” and “to protect consumer data from unauthorized access and exfiltration.” Doc. 1 at 10.

 8   Defendants have established that their cyber security management and mitigation efforts largely

 9   occurred in the Northern District of Texas. See Doc. 15-2. There is no comparable connection to this

10   district. Where, as here, “the transactions giving rise to the action lack a significant connection to the

11   plaintiff’s chosen forum, the plaintiff’s choice of forum is given considerably less weight.” Hawkins,

12   924 F. Supp. 2d at 1215.

13          Plaintiff argues that his choice of forum is nonetheless entitled to deference because he is a

14   California resident, he limited the proposed class to only California plaintiffs, and he alleges solely a

15   California statutory cause of action. Doc. 18 at 8. A plaintiff’s choice of forum in a class action may

16   be entitled to some deference where a plaintiff has chosen the forum of his primary residence and not

17   engaged in forum shopping. White v. GlaxoSmithKline Consumer Healthcare Holdings (US) LLC,

18   No. 20-cv-04048-SVK, 2020 WL 13578444, at *3 (N.D. Cal. Nov. 17, 2020) (citing In re Ferrero

19   Litig., 768 F. Supp. 2d 1074, 1078-79 (S.D. Cal. 2011)). However, here the Cabezas action was filed

20   several months before Plaintiff filed suit, and the putative nationwide class in the consolidated action

21   appears to include all the members in Plaintiff’s proposed California class. Plaintiff defines his

22   California class as “[a]ll persons residing in California whose nonredacted and nonencrypted personal

23   information was compromised in the data breach(es) affecting Mr. Cooper’s network(s) in 2023.”

24   Doc. 1 at 12. Plaintiff and the proposed California class members are included in the Cabezas action’s

25   broad definition of its nationwide class as “[a]ll ascertainable persons” impacted by that same data

26   breach, “including all who were sent a notice of the Data Breach.” Doc. 18-1 at 12. Additionally,

27   Defendants note that California residents are already serving as named plaintiffs in the consolidated

28

                                                          6
       Case 1:24-cv-00177-KES-SKO Document 31 Filed 04/26/24 Page 7 of 12


 1   action in the Northern District of Texas. Doc. 24 at 10. Moreover, the consolidated Cabezas action

 2   will continue in the Northern District of Texas regardless of the outcome of this transfer motion.

 3          Plaintiff fails to provide a persuasive reason for proceeding separately in this district rather

 4   than transfer this case to the Northern District of Texas, where it could be consolidated with the

 5   Cabezas action. See Hawkins, 924 F. Supp. 2d at 1215 (finding transfer appropriate where there were

 6   no persuasive reasons to artificially split class into two sub-classes: the California plaintiffs and the

 7   non-California “nationwide” class). Accordingly, this factor supports a venue transfer.

 8                  3.      The parties’ contacts with the forum and contacts relating to plaintiff’s cause

 9                          of action

10          Plaintiff argues that he resides in this district, has no contacts with Texas, and negotiated his

11   loan with Defendants in this district. Doc. 18 at 6. He asserts that his proposed plaintiff class is

12   similarly situated. Id. at 7. While Plaintiff resides in the Eastern District of California, the crux of the

13   case has little to do with this district. The fact that Defendants engaged in a loan transaction with

14   Plaintiff in California does not establish that any significant relevant conduct occurred in this district.

15          The material allegations in Plaintiff’s complaint concern conduct outside of California, largely

16   in the Northern District of Texas. Defendants have presented evidence that their principal place of

17   business is in the Northern District of Texas, that their key personnel responsible for managing data

18   security and responding to the cyberattack are based in that district, and that their operations and

19   technology systems are largely located in, or run from, that district. Doc. 15-2. In contrast, Plaintiff

20   fails to identify any relationship between the Eastern District of California and the conduct and

21   evidence concerning Defendants’ cyber security management and the data breach, which form the basis

22   for plaintiff’s claim. See Doc. 1. Accordingly, these factors support a venue transfer.

23                  4.      Ease of access to evidence

24          Defendants note that their primary information technology team, documents, and customer

25   records are in the Northern District of Texas. Doc. 15-2. Defendants’ operations and technology

26   systems are in, or run from, their principal executive office in Coppell, which is in the Northern

27   District of Texas, Dallas Division. Id. Plaintiff argues the location of the evidence is of minimal

28   importance because, even if some transportation of documents is required, the evidence is likely in an

                                                          7
       Case 1:24-cv-00177-KES-SKO Document 31 Filed 04/26/24 Page 8 of 12


 1   electronic format and technological advances in document storage and retrieval have greatly reduced

 2   the burden associated with the location of evidence. Doc. 18 at 11.

 3          Ease of access to evidence is generally not a predominate concern in evaluating whether to

 4   transfer venue because “advances in technology have made it easy for documents to be transferred to

 5   different locations.” Byler v. Deluxe Corp., 222 F. Supp. 3d 885, 906-07 (S.D. Cal. 2016). The

 6   evidence identified by Defendants is likely available in an electronic format easily transferrable

 7   between the parties and would almost certainly have to be transferred electronically in any matter.

 8   Nonetheless, nearly all identified witnesses and relevant sources of proof are in the Northern District

 9   of Texas. On balance, access to such evidence would be somewhat easier in the Texas district. For

10   these reasons, this factor weighs somewhat in favor of transfer.

11                  5.      Cost of litigation and time to trial

12          Given that the relevant witnesses and evidence are largely in the Northern District of Texas,

13   and that Plaintiff does not identify any significant evidentiary connection to this district, the Court

14   agrees with Defendants that trying the case in the Texas court would be less burdensome, costly, and

15   difficult for the parties. Additionally, courts consider “the administrative difficulties flowing from

16   court congestion” in considering the interests of justice. Decker Coal, 805 F.2d at 843 (citing Piper

17   Aircraft, 454 U.S. at 255). Defendants note that, as of December 2023, the Eastern District of

18   California had an average of 883 cases per judgeship and a median of 68.3 months from filing to trial

19   for civil cases, while the Northern District of Texas has 584 cases per judgeship and a median of 27.3

20   months from filing to trial for civil cases. Doc. 15-1 at 16. These considerations similarly weigh

21   somewhat in favor of transfer.

22                  6.      Convenience of witnesses

23          Convenience of witnesses is one of the most important factors in the determination of whether

24   to grant a change of venue. See, e.g., Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum Comm’n v. Nat’l Football

25   League, 89 F.R.D. 497, 501 (C.D. Cal. 1981). A transfer of venue “may be denied when witnesses

26   either live in the forum district or are within the 100-mile reach of the subpoena power” because

27   individuals cannot be compelled to testify when they reside beyond the boundaries of the Court’s

28   subpoena power. Id. at 501 (citing Fed. R. Civ. P. 45(e); U.S. Industries, Inc. v. Procter & Gamble Co.,

                                                          8
       Case 1:24-cv-00177-KES-SKO Document 31 Filed 04/26/24 Page 9 of 12


 1   348 F. Supp. 1265 (S.D.N.Y. 1972)). “To demonstrate inconvenience of witnesses, the moving party

 2   must identify relevant witnesses, state their location and describe their testimony and its relevance.”

 3   Carolina Cas. Co. v. Data Broad Corp., 158 F. Supp. 2d 1044, 1049 (N.D. Cal. 2001); see also E. & J.

 4   Gallo Winery v. F. & P. S.p.A., 899 F. Supp. 465, 466 (E.D. Cal. 1994) (“[a]ffidavits or declarations are

 5   required to identify key witnesses and a generalized statement of their anticipated testimony”). Here,

 6   neither party has identified any non-party witnesses by location and relevant testimony. However,

 7   Defendants have established that its witnesses with knowledge of Defendant’s security measures and

 8   the data breach are largely in the Northern District of Texas, whereas there are no comparable identified

 9   witnesses in this district. Accordingly, this factor on balance supports transfer.

10                   7.      Familiarity with governing law

11           Plaintiff argues this Court is more familiar with his California law claim than the Texas federal

12   court and asserts this as a factor weighing against transfer. Doc. 18 at 7. This argument is

13   unpersuasive. “[C]ourts have repeatedly explained that the forum state's familiarity with California law

14   is a neutral factor in a § 1404(a) transfer analysis, because ‘it is routine for federal courts to analyze and

15   apply the laws of diverse states.’” Kitzler v. Nelnet Servicing, LLC, No. CV 22-6550-MWF (KS), 2022

16   WL 18284983, at *3 (C.D. Cal. Nov. 1, 2022). The Northern District of Texas court is fully capable of

17   deciding issues arising under California law. See Metz v. U.S. Life Ins. Co., 674 F. Supp. 2d 1141,

18   1148 (C.D. Cal. 2009) (judges in both New York and California are fully capable of deciding issues

19   arising under both California and New York law); Saunders v. USAA Life Ins. Co., 71 F. Supp. 3d

20   1058, 1061 (N.D. Cal. 2014) (it is routine for federal courts to analyze and apply the law of diverse

21   states). This factor is therefore neutral.

22                   8.      Public interest

23           Plaintiff also asserts that the public interest weighs against transfer, arguing that the Northern

24   District of Texas has no interest in protecting California citizens under a California statute. Id. at 12.

25   This argument is unpersuasive. The Northern District of Texas is equally able to protect Plaintiff and

26   the putative California class members in this matter. See Parr v. Stevens Transp., Inc., No. C-19-

27   02610-WHA, 2019 WL 4933583 *4 (N.D. Cal. Oct. 7, 2019) (finding in a wage-and-hour class action

28   “the excellent judges in Dallas can apply California law, and can appreciate and vindicate California's

                                                          9
      Case 1:24-cv-00177-KES-SKO Document 31 Filed 04/26/24 Page 10 of 12


 1   interest in the matter.”) Moreover, the Northern District of Texas already has before it the Cabezas

 2   putative nationwide class action, which includes Plaintiff and the other putative California class

 3   members, as well as the numerous other cases consolidated under Cabezas. Additionally, the operative

 4   facts in this action occurred in the Northern District of Texas, and Texas has an equal or greater interest

 5   in policing the conduct of the companies headquartered within that state. See N. Bay Credit Union v.

 6   MRB Direct, No. 8:23-cv-01951, 2024 WL 1136137, at *3 (C.D. Cal. Jan. 26, 2024) (“while

 7   recognizing California's interest in protecting its residents and businesses . . . Nevada courts have at

 8   least an equal interest in hearing cases involving Nevada businesses.”). For these reasons, this factor

 9   supports transfer.

10           For the foregoing reasons, the Court finds transfer to the Northern District of Texas is

11   appropriate under 28 U.S.C. § 1404(a).

12           B.      First-to-File Rule

13           The Court also finds it appropriate to consider the first-to-file rule as an independent and

14   alternative basis for transfer. A district court examines three factors in deciding whether to apply the

15   first-to-file rule: “chronology of the lawsuits, similarity of the parties, and similarity of the issues.”

16   Kohn Law Grp., Inc. v. Auto Parts Mfg. Mississippi, Inc., 787 F.3d 1237, 1240 (9th Cir. 2015).

17                   1.      Chronology of the actions

18           “The first factor simply requires a chronology of the actions.” Young v. L’Oreal USA, Inc.,

19   526 F. Supp. 3d 700, 704 (N.D. Cal. 2021). Plaintiff does not dispute that the Cabezas action was

20   filed first. Doc. 18 at 13. Accordingly, this factor weighs in favor of applying the first-to-file rule.

21                   2.      Similarity of the parties

22           This second factor requires merely that the parties be substantially similar; they need not be

23   identical. Kohn Law Grp., 787 F.3d at 1240. In the class action context, district courts in this circuit

24   generally compare the putative classes rather than the named plaintiffs. See Young, 526 F. Supp. 3d at

25   705; Wallerstein, 967 F. Supp. 2d at 1295; Pedro v. Millennium Prods., Inc., No. 15-cv-05253-MMC,

26   2016 WL 3029681, at *3 (N.D. Cal. May 27, 2016) (collecting cases).

27           As addressed above, Plaintiff and the California class members he seeks to represent are

28   members of the putative nationwide class in the consolidated Cabezas action. Plaintiff’s filing on

                                                          10
      Case 1:24-cv-00177-KES-SKO Document 31 Filed 04/26/24 Page 11 of 12


 1   behalf of a California-only class does not create dissimilarity between the parties in this action and

 2   those in the consolidated Cabezas action. See Young, 526 F. Supp. 3d at 705 (parties similar where

 3   California plaintiff class in second action were members of nationwide class in first action); Koehler v.

 4   Pepperidge Farm, Inc., No. 13-cv-2644-YGR, 2013 WL 4806895, at *4 (N.D. Cal. Sept. 9, 2013)

 5   (parties similar where first-filed action sought to represent nationwide class and subsequent case

 6   sought to represent only California class); Wallerstein, 967 F. Supp. 2d at 1296 (finding substantially

 7   similar parties where both classes sought to represent at least some of the same individuals).

 8          Accordingly, the Court finds there is substantial similarity between the parties involved in both

 9   cases, and the second requirement of the rule is satisfied.

10                  3.      Similarity of the issues

11          The final factor is whether the actions involve similar issues. Courts have held that “the issues

12   between the two cases need not be identical, only substantially similar.” Young, 526 F. Supp. 3d at

13   705; see also Wallerstein, 967 F. Supp. 2d at 1296; Kohn Law Grp., 787 F.3d at 1240. Defendants

14   assert the actions clearly raise similar issues and claims concerning the data breach and Mr. Cooper’s

15   alleged actions, or failure to act, with respect to securely maintaining the putative class members’

16   personal information. Doc. 15 at 20. Plaintiff argues that, nonetheless, the actions are not similar

17   because his action asserts solely a CCPA claim, while the Cabezas action does not include such a

18   claim. Doc. 18 at 15. The Court agrees with Defendants that the legal issues and remedies in the

19   cases are substantially similar. Plaintiff’s sole claim, a CCPA violation, raises factual and legal issues

20   substantially similar at least to those at issue in the negligence claim in the consolidated Cabezas

21   action. The core factual issues concern the same data breach and Mr. Cooper’s alleged violation of its

22   duty to maintain reasonable security procedures.

23          Similarly, in Wallerstein the court found two lawsuits to be substantially similar where both

24   cases arose from the same alleged mislabeling by the defendant, even though the plaintiff in the

25   second-filed case brought additional claims under New York law while the initial action raised only

26   California claims. Wallerstein, 967 F. Supp. 2d at 1297. In Schwartz v. Frito-Lay N. Am., No. C-12-

27   02740 EDL, 2012 WL 8147135, at *3 (N.D. Cal. Sept. 12, 2012), the court likewise found two actions

28   to be substantially similar where both cases arose from the same allegedly misleading conduct by the

                                                         11
      Case 1:24-cv-00177-KES-SKO Document 31 Filed 04/26/24 Page 12 of 12


 1   defendant. The court found that a contrary holding would undermine the purpose of the first-to-file

 2   rule. Id. (citing Worthington v. Bayer Healthcare, LLC, No. 11-3017, 2012 WL 1079716, at *6

 3   (D.N.J. Mar. 30, 2012) (“Finding an insubstantial overlap because of the fact that the claims are

 4   asserted under different state laws would defeat the judicial efficiency rationale undergirding the first-

 5   filed rule.”)).

 6            For these reasons, the Court finds, in the alternative, that all three requirements of the first-to-

 7   file rule are met and that the present case should be transferred to the Northern District of Texas under

 8   that rule.

 9   IV.      Conclusion and Order

10            Based upon the foregoing, the Defendants have met their burden of demonstrating that a

11   transfer of venue is appropriate under 28 U.S.C. § 1404(a). The court further finds, in the alternative,

12   that transfer of venue would be appropriate under the first-to-file rule.

13            Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to transfer venue, Doc. 15, is GRANTED.

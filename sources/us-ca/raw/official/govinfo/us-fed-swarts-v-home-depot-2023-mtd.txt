                                  22   jurisdiction is proper. Boschetto v. Hansing, 539 F.3d 1011, 1015 (9th Cir. 2008). “Where, as

                                  23   here, a motion to dismiss is based on written materials rather than an evidentiary hearing, the

                                  24   plaintiff need only make a prima facie showing of jurisdictional facts.” Love v. Associated

                                  25   Newspapers, Ltd., 611 F.3d 601, 608 (9th Cir. 2010). To make this showing, “the plaintiff need

                                   6          Federal courts recognize two types of personal jurisdiction over a defendant: general and

                                   7   specific. Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v. Superior Ct. of California, San Francisco Cnty., 582 U.S.

                                   8   255, 262 (2017) (citation omitted). “A court with general jurisdiction may hear any claim against

                                   9   that defendant, even if all the incidents underlying the claim occurred in a different [s]tate.” Id.

                                  10   (emphasis and citation omitted). General jurisdiction arises “when [the defendant’s] affiliations

                                  11   with the [s]tate are so ‘continuous and systematic’ as to render them essentially at home in the

                                  14   home” only in its place of incorporation and its principal place of business. Ford Motor Co. v.

                                  15   Montana Eighth Jud. Dist. Ct., 141 S. Ct. 1017, 1024 (2021).

                                  16          By contrast, specific jurisdiction arises when a defendant’s contacts with the forum give

                                  17   rise to the claim in question. See Daimler AG v. Bauman, 571 U.S. 117, 188 (2014). “In other

                                  18   words, there must be ‘an affiliation between the forum and the underlying controversy, principally,

                                  19   [an] activity or an occurrence that takes place in the forum [s]tate and is therefore subject to the

                                  20   State's regulation.’” Id. (quoting Goodyear Dunlop Tires Operations, S.A. v. Brown, 564 U.S.

                                  21   915, 919 (2011)).

                                  22          “The general rule is that personal jurisdiction over a defendant is proper if it is permitted

                                  23   by a long-arm statute and if the exercise of that jurisdiction does not violate federal due process.”

                                  24   Pebble Beach Co. v. Caddy, 453 F.3d 1151, 1154–55 (9th Cir. 2006) (citing Fireman’s Fund Ins.

                                  25   Co. v. Nat. Bank of Coops., 103 F.3d 888, 893 (9th Cir. 1996)). “Where, as here, no federal

                                  26   statute authorizes personal jurisdiction, the district court applies the law of the state in which the

                                  27   court sits.” Mavrix Photo, Inc. v. Brand Techs., Inc., 647 F.3d 1218, 1223 (9th Cir. 2011).

                                  16           B.      Rule 12(b)(6)

                                  17           To survive a motion to dismiss under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6), a

                                  18   complaint must contain “a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is

                                  19   entitled to relief.” Fed. R. Civ. P. 8(a)(2). Dismissal “is appropriate only where the complaint

                                  20   lacks a cognizable legal theory or sufficient facts to support a cognizable legal theory.”

                                  21   Mendiondo v. Centinela Hosp. Med. Ctr., 521 F.3d 1097, 1104 (9th Cir. 2008). “[A] complaint

                                  22   must contain sufficient factual matter, accepted as true, to ‘state a claim to relief that is plausible

                                  23   on its face.’” Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662, 678 (2009) (quoting Bell Atl. Corp. v. Twombly,

                                  24   550 U.S. 544, 570 (2007)). Factual allegations need not be detailed, but facts must be “enough to

                                  25   raise a right to relief above the speculative level.” Twombly, 550 U.S. at 555.

                                  26           “A claim has facial plausibility when the plaintiff pleads factual content that allows the

                                  27   court to draw the reasonable inference that the defendant is liable for the misconduct

                                  28   alleged.” Iqbal, 556 U.S. at 678. While this standard is not “akin to a ‘probability
                                                                                           4
                                        Case 4:23-cv-00995-JST           Document 31          Filed 08/30/23    Page 5 of 17




                                   1   requirement,’ . . . it asks for more than a sheer possibility that a defendant has acted unlawfully.”

                                   2   Id. (quoting Twombly, 550 U.S. at 556). “Where a complaint pleads facts that are ‘merely

                                   3   consistent with’ a defendant’s liability, it ‘stops short of the line between possibility and

                                   4   plausibility of entitlement to relief.’” Id. (quoting Twombly, 550 U.S. at 557). In determining

                                   5   whether a plaintiff has met the plausibility requirement, a court must “accept all factual allegations

                                   6   in the complaint as true and construe the pleadings in the light most favorable” to the

                                   7   plaintiff. Knievel v. ESPN, 393 F.3d 1068, 1072 (9th Cir. 2005). A plaintiff may “plead[] facts

                                   8   alleged upon information and belief where the facts are peculiarly within the possession and

                                   9   control of the defendant or where the belief is based on factual information that makes the

                                  10   inference of culpability plausible.” Soo Park v. Thompson, 851 F.3d 910, 928 (9th Cir. 2017)

                                  11   (quoting Arista Records, LLC v. Doe 3, 603 F.3d 110, 120 (2d Cir. 2010)).

                                  25                          a.      Purposeful Direction
                                  26          The first prong of the minimum contact test is often called the “purposeful availment”

                                  27   prong. Yahoo! Inc. v. La Ligue Contre Le Racisme Et L’Antisemitisme, 433 F.3d 1199, 1206 (9th

                                  28   Cir. 2006). The phrase “purposeful availment,” however, is often used “in shorthand fashion, to
                                                                                          5
                                        Case 4:23-cv-00995-JST           Document 31          Filed 08/30/23     Page 6 of 17




                                   1   include both purposeful availment and purposeful direction.” Schwarzenegger, 374 F.3d at 802.

                                   2   “A purposeful availment analysis is most often used in suits sounding in contract.” Id. (citation

                                   3   omitted). “A purposeful direction analysis, on the other hand, is most often used in suits sounding

                                   4   in tort.” Id. (citation omitted). Because Plaintiff’s CIPA and Wiretap Act claims sound in tort,

                                   5   and their UCL claim is derivative of those two claims, the Court applies a purposeful direction

                                   6   analysis. See S.D. v. Hytto Ltd., No. 18-CV-00688-JSW, 2019 WL 8333519, at *3 (N.D. Cal.

                                   7   May 15, 2019) (applying purposeful direction analysis to plaintiffs’ Wiretap Act, intrusion upon

                                   8   seclusion, and unjust enrichment claims).

                                   9           In applying a purposeful direction analysis, “[courts] typically inquire whether a defendant

                                  10   ‘purposefully direct[s] his activities’ at the forum state, applying an ‘effects’ test that focuses on

                                  11   the forum in which the defendant’s actions were felt, whether or not the actions themselves

                                  12   occurred within the forum.” Yahoo!, 433 F.3d at 1206 (citing Calder v. Jones, 465 U.S. 783, 789–


 United States District Court
                                  13   90 (1984)). The effects test includes three elements: “the defendant allegedly must have (1)

                                  14   committed an intentional act, (2) expressly aimed at the forum state, (3) causing harm that the

                                  15   defendant knows is likely to be suffered in the forum state.” AMA Multimedia, Ltd. Liab. Co. v.



Northern District of California
                                  16   Wanat, 970 F.3d 1201, 1208–09 (9th Cir. 2020) (quoting Mavrix Photo, 647 F.3d at 1228).

                                  17           It is undisputed that Home Depot operates its own website and chat function, and thus,

                                  18   Swarts’s claims satisfy the first element. A defendant acts intentionally when he acts with “an

                                  19   intent to perform an actual, physical act in the real world, rather than an intent to accomplish a

                                  20   result or consequence of that act.” Schwarzenegger, 374 F.3d at 806. “[O]perating a passive

                                  21   website, purchasing a domain name and purchasing domain privacy services are all intentional

                                  22   acts.” Will Co., Ltd. v. Lee, 47 F.4th 917, 922 (9th Cir. 2022) (citing AMA Multimedia, 970 F.3d

                                  23   at 1209). With regard to the third element, “[a] defendant causes harm in a particular forum when

                                  24   the ‘bad acts’ that form the basis of the plaintiff’s complaint occur in that forum.” Id. at 926

                                  25   (quoting Mavrix Photo, 647 F.3d at 1231). Here, taking Plaintiff’s allegations as true, Home

                                  26   Depot was aware that it was recording its online chat conversations with customers, which could

                                  27   foreseeably cause harm in California. ECF No. 17 ¶¶ 17, 25. The third element is therefore

                                  28   satisfied.

                                   2          Understanding Home Depot’s argument that LivePerson, and other third parties, are

                                   3   merely tools “supplying [recorded] information back to Defendant” requires a brief explanation of

                                   4   two cases that have shaped § 631 jurisprudence. ECF No. 23 at 20. In Rogers v. Ulrich, Ulrich

                                   5   used a tape-recording device to record a conversation with Rogers without his knowledge or

                                   6   consent. 52 Cal. App. 3d 894, 897 (1975). In holding for Ulrich, the court looked to the

                                   7   Legislature’s intent in passing CIPA and noted that “it speaks of preventing eavesdropping . . .

                                   8   thus suggesting that participant recording was not meant to be included.” Id. at 899. In turn, the

                                   9   court reasoned that “[i]t is never a secret to one party to a conversation that the other party is

                                  10   listening to a conversation; only a third party can listen secretly to a private conversation.” Id.

                                  11          In Ribas v. Clark, after Ribas and his wife finalized their divorce settlement, the wife asked

                                  12   her friend, Clark, to listen in on a conversation with her former husband via an extension. 38 Cal.


 United States District Court
                                  13   3d 355, 358 (1985). In determining that the friend’s actions violated § 631, the California

                                  14   Supreme Court reasoned that “a substantial distinction has been recognized between the

                                  15   secondhand repetition of the contents of a conversation and its simultaneous dissemination to an



Northern District of California
                                  16   unannounced second auditor, whether that auditor be a person or mechanical device.” Id. at 361.

                                  17   The Court specifically held that “such secret monitoring denies the speaker an important aspect of

                                  18   privacy of communication—the right to control the nature and extent of the firsthand

                                  19   dissemination of his statements.” Id.

                                  20          Following the Ribas and Rogers distinction, the determinative question for courts is

                                  21   whether the third parties at issue are “more akin to the tape recorder in Rogers,” “or [to] the friend

                                  22   in Ribas.” Javier, 2023 WL 114225, at *5. As the court stated persuasively in Javier, this

                                  23   distinction turns on the “question of whether the third party ‘ha[s] the capability to use its record

                                  24   of the interaction for any other purpose.’” Yockey v. Salesforce, Inc., No. 22-CV-09067-JST, 2023

                                  25   WL 5519323, at *5 (N.D. Cal. Aug. 25, 2023) (quoting Javier, 2023 WL 114225, at *6). Thus,

                                  26   “[i]f [Liveperson] can use its records of the communications for any purpose other than to furnish

                                  27   information relating to those communications to [Home Depot], then it is more like the wife in

                                  28   Ribas who allowed Clark to eavesdrop. By contrast, if [Liveperson] “has no independent ability to
                                                                                          12

                                  21          D.      Wiretap Act

                                  22          Swarts’s third claim is based upon a violation of the Wiretap Act. The Wiretap Act

                                  23   provides for civil penalties against any person who “intentionally intercepts, endeavors to

                                  24   intercept, or procures any other person to intercept or endeavor to intercept, any wire, oral, or

                                  25   electronic communication.” 18 U.S.C. § 2511(1)(a). “The analysis for a violation of CIPA is the

                                  26   same as that under the federal Wiretap Act.” NovelPoster, 140 F. Supp. 3d at 954. Because

                                  27   Swarts is unable to allege that Home Depot intercepted the communications in transit under the

                                  28   CIPA, he is also unable to allege a violation of the Wiretap Act.

                                   1          E.      UCL

                                   2          Swarts’s final claim alleges a violation of California’s Unfair Competition Law. To bring

                                   3   a UCL claim, a person must have “suffered injury in fact and . . . lost money or property as a result

                                   4   of the unfair competition.” Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17204. Thus, a UCL plaintiff must “(1)

                                   5   establish a loss or deprivation of money or property sufficient to qualify as injury in fact, i.e.,

                                   6   economic injury, and (2) show that that economic injury was the result of, i.e., caused by, the

                                   7   unfair business practice or false advertising that is the gravamen of the claim.” Kwikset Corp. v.

                                   8   Superior Court, 51 Cal. 4th 310, 322 (2011) (emphasis in original).

                                   9          Home Depot argues that Swarts does not allege any economic loss, and states only that his

                                  10   “right to privacy is being infringed.” ECF No. 23 at 25. Swarts counters that he has UCL

                                  11   standing because he “only seeks injunctive relief and need not satisfy the UCL’s requirements for

                                  12   seeking restitution.” ECF No. 28 at 10. The sole case Swarts cites in support of this position is


 United States District Court
                                  13   White v. Trans Union, LLC, 462 F. Supp. 2d 1079 (C.D. Cal. 2006). The Court finds this reliance

                                  14   misplaced. The White court held that the plaintiff need not demonstrate that the defendant directly

                                  15   took money from him in order to establish standing to sue for injunctive relief under the UCL. Id.



Northern District of California
                                  16   at 1083. As another court explained:

                                  17                  White does not stand for the proposition that a UCL plaintiff need
                                                      not show he has suffered injury in fact to demonstrate that he has
                                  18                  standing to sue for injunctive relief. Rather, it simply recognizes
                                                      that a plaintiff need not allege that the defendant directly took
                                  19                  money from him—i.e., allege recoverable restitution—in order to
                                                      allege a loss of money or property sufficient to confer standing to
                                  20                  sue for a UCL violation.
                                  21   Bontrager v. Showmark Media LLC, No. CV 14-01144 MMM-EX, 2014 WL 12600201, at *8

                                  22   (C.D. Cal. June 20, 2014)

                                  23          At bottom, Swarts cannot circumvent the requirement that he has lost money or property.

                                  24   Plaintiff cites to Calhoun v. Google LLC, 526 F. Supp. 3d 605, 636 (N.D. Cal. 2021) to support his

                                  25   position that a loss of personal information can confer standing. However, as another court in this

                                  26   District observed, Calhoun rests on four cases that address Article III standing, which is different

                                  27   from standing under the UCL. See Cottle v. Plaid Inc., 536 F. Supp. 3d 461, 484 n.8 (N.D. Cal.

                                  28   2021) (disagreeing with Calhoun). Numerous courts have held that disclosure of personal
                                                                                          16
                                       Case 4:23-cv-00995-JST          Document 31        Filed 08/30/23      Page 17 of 17




                                   1   information alone does not constitute economic or property loss sufficient to establish UCL

                                   2   standing, unless the plaintiff provides specific allegations regarding the value of the information.

                                   3   See, e.g., In re Yahoo! Inc. Customer Data Sec. Breach Litig., No. 16-MD-02752-LHK, 2017 WL

                                   4   3727318, at *22 (N.D. Cal. Aug. 30, 2017) (rejecting UCL standing to victims of data breach who

                                   5   had failed to allege specific benefit-of-the-bargain losses or out-of-pocket expenses); In re

                                   6   Facebook Privacy Litig., 791 F. Supp. 2d 705, 714 (N.D. Cal. 2011), aff’d 572 F. App’x 494 (9th

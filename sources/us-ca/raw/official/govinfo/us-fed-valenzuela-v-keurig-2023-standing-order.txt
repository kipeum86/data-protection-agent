                                  13          Pending before the Court is Defendant’s motion to dismiss the Second Amended

                                  14   Complaint (SAC). Federal courts are obligated to ensure standing exists and to raise it “sua sponte

                                  15   if need be.” San Francisco Drydock, Inc. v. Dalton, 131 F.3d 776, 778 (9th Cir. 1997); see also



Northern District of California
                                  16   Ctr. For Biological Diversity v. Kempthorne, 588 F.3d 701, 707 (9th Cir. 2009) (“[T]he

                                  17   jurisdictional issue of standing can be raised at any time.”). Upon review of the SAC and the

                                  18   parties’ submissions, the Court has questions as to whether Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged

                                  19   Article III standing.

                                  20          Article III standing requires more than a statutory violation; a plaintiff must establish

                                  21   “concrete harm.” TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez, 141 S. Ct. 2190, 2205 (2021). The “named

                                  22   plaintiffs who represent a class must allege and show that they personally have been injured, not

                                  23   that injury has been suffered by other, unidentified members of the class to which they belong.”

                                  24   Spokeo, Inc. v. Robins, 578 U.S. 330, 338 n.6 (2016), as revised (May 24, 2016) (cleaned up). In

                                  25   Mikulsky v. Noom, Inc., for example, the court found no injury-in-fact in a putative class action

                                  26   based on the California Invasion of Privacy Act because the plaintiff did not “identify the specific

                                  27   personal information she disclosed that implicates a protectable privacy interest.” No. 3:23-CV-

                                  28   00285-H-MSB, 2023 WL 4567096, at *5 (S.D. Cal. July 17, 2023) (cleaned up). The court
                                        Case 3:22-cv-09042-JSC            Document 53         Filed 09/01/23     Page 2 of 2




                                   1   explained, “Plaintiff’s conclusory allegation that she disclosed ‘personal information’ does not

                                   2   allow the Court to determine whether Plaintiff has a protectable privacy interest in that

                                   3   information” since “basic contact information, such as [] email address or phone number” would

                                   4   be insufficient to establish Article III standing. Id.; see also I.C. v. Zynga, Inc., 600 F. Supp. 3d

                                   5   1034, 1049 (N.D. Cal. 2022) (“[T]he Court is hard pressed to conclude that basic contact

                                   6   information, including one's email address, phone number . . . is private information.”); Byars v.

                                   7   Sterling Jewelers, Inc., No. 5:22-CV-01456-SB-SP, 2023 WL 2996686, at *3 (C.D. Cal. Apr. 5,

                                   8   2023) (concluding the plaintiff did not allege any concrete injury in a similar case when the

                                   9   plaintiff failed to “allege that she disclosed any sensitive information to Defendant”). Here,

                                  10   Plaintiff does not allege what, if anything she disclosed; thus, the Court is not yet satisfied

                                  11   Plaintiff has alleged a concrete harm sufficient to support constitutional standing.

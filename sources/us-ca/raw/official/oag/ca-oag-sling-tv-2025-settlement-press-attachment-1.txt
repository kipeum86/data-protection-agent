 1         The People of the State of California, through Attorney General Rob Bonta, bring this

 2   action against Defendants Sling TV L.L.C. and Dish Media Sales L.L.C . (collectively "S ling

 4   facts based on information and belief:

 5                                            INTRODUCTION
 6         1.    Californians enjoy strong privacy protections under the California Consumer Privacy

 7   Act ("CCPA")-especially the right to direct a business to stop selling or sharing their personal

 8   information, known as "the right to opt-out." The CCPA right to opt-out gives consumers

 9   meaningful control over how their personal information is used, especially in an online

10   environment where personal data is collected, shared, and sold in ways that may not be obvious to

11   a consumer. The right to opt-out is broad and applies to both online and offline data selling and

12   sharing, and must be easy to execute and require minimal steps. Businesses should not bury the

13   CCPA opt out with other choices, like cookie preferences, that do not provide the same broad

14   directive to stop selling or sharing data. Conflating or combining a consumer' s right to opt-out

15   with cookie choices or using other design elements that require multiple steps, unnecessarily

16   request additional information, or prompt confirmation can be confusing or add unnecessary

17   burden for consumers seeking to exercise their privacy rights.

18         2.    Sling TV is an internet-based live TV and media streaming service that collects and

19   processes consumer personal information in connection with targeted adve1iising on its platform.

20   Sling TV deceptively directed consumers seeking to exercise their CCPA right to opt-out to its

21   cookie preference controls-even though cookies are not the only way Sling TV sells and shares

22   consumers' data. Consumers who figured out that turning off cookies would not stop all selling

23   and sharing of their information had to click through hard-to-find links and fill out a webform to

24   actually opt out. Even logged-in customers, where Sling TV already had the information

25   necessary to stop selling or sharing the customer' s personal information, were required to provide

26   additional information. Sling TV also did not provide an opt-out method within its apps, even

27   though most Sling TV customers use the app on various connected living-room devices to access

28   its services. Instead, Sling TV customers were required to type in a complicated URL in a
                                                      2

 1   separate web browser and work through several additional steps. In doing so, Sling TV violated

 2   the CCPA by failing to provide consumers with easy-to-use methods to opt out of selling and

 3   sharing of their personal information.

 4         3.    Unlike other streaming services, while Sling TV allowed users to create user profiles

 5   by asking " Who ' s Watching?", it did not offer parents the ability to set one or more user profiles

 6   as a " kid ' s profile" that would eliminate or limit the use of targeted advertising when children are

 7   watching. Nor did Sling TV otherwise age-screen users, default off personalized adve11ising, or

 8   obtain affirmative "opt-in" authorization when children likely were watching on shared household

 9   devices. For these and other reasons, Sling TV did not implement sufficient protections for the

10   data of children watching Sling TV.

 4         8.    Sling TV was the first internet-based live TV service. Sling TV operates an app-

 5   based, live television and streaming service that lets consumers watch spot1s, movies, and shows

 6   live or on demand over the internet. Sling TV offers both paid subscriptions and a free ad-

 8   subscription and Freestream versions. Sling TV operates a website at www.sling.com and the

 9   Sling TV application on various living room devices including sma11/connected TVs, mobile

10   devices, and gaming consoles.

11         9.    Sling TV ' s revenue includes both subscription fees and advet1ising revenue. Unlike

12   traditional cable and broadcast TV service, Sling TV's internet-based streaming service engages

13   in targeted advertising using the personal information of its customers similar to targeted

14   advet1ising on the internet. Sling TV sells or shares consumer personal information in connection

15   with targeted advertising.

16         10.   To enhance its profits, Sling TV buys data about its customers, improving its targeted

17   adve11ising capabilities and making its ad spots more valuable to its advet1ising clients. Sling TV

18   purchases, licenses, or otherwise collects data about its customers and their households from

19   various data brokers and third-party vendors based on activity across businesses, distinctly

20   branded internet websites, applications, or services other than Sling TV (third-party data). The

21   third-party data includes detailed information such as age, gender, income, locati on, interests,

22   party affiliation, home and car ownership, and other detailed demographic and psychographic

23   attributes. Sling TV combines this third-party data about its customers with the data it has directly

24   from its customers (first-patty data) to create an enhanced data set for targeted adve11ising

25   purposes. This allows Sling TV to maximize its advet1ising profits, providing advet1isers the

26   valuable oppot1unity to precisely target consumers on its platform. This is a form of "cross-

27   context behavioral adve11ising" as defined in the CCPA, Civil Code, section 1798.140,

28   subdivision (k).

     1         I. Sling TV Violated the CCPA By Not Providing Easy-to-Execute Methods for
                  Consumers to Opt Out of the Selling and Sharing of Their Personal Information.
     2

     3         11.   Sling TV made interface design choices that made it difficult for consumers seeking

     4   to effectuate their right to opt-out. Instead of a "Do Not Sell My Personal Information" link, Sling

     5   TV posted a "Your Privacy Choices" link, which directed consumers to cookie preferences. In

     6   this context, cookie preferences are not the same as a CCPA opt-out of the sale and sharing of a

     7   consumer's personal information. Cookie choices typically include options to allow, limit, or
     8   refuse cookies placed in a browser or to manage similar tracking technologies on an internet

     9   website. But the CCPA opt-out right is broader: it permits a consumer to direct the business to

    10   stop all cookie-based and non-cookie-based selling and sharing of the consumer's personal

    11   information across browsers, devices, and offline. Sling TV's cookie choices did not operate as a

    12   CCPA opt-out directing the business to stop all selling and sharing of the consumer's personal

    13   information.

    14         12.   Consumers who figured out that rejecting advertising and analytics cookies would not

    15   give them the protections mandated by the CCPA had to locate a link embedded in text that

    16   directed them to a separate opt-out page. To make matters worse, in one version Sling TV

-   17   deceptively characterized cookie choices as "Do Not Sell/Share My Info," indicated "Opted In"

    18   when cookies were allowed and "Opted Out" when cookies were turned off, and required

    19   consumers to find and click on an unlabeled caret to reveal the opt-out webform. This further led

    20   consumers to believe that merely turning off cookies was sufficient to effectuate their CCPA

    21   rights, added additional unnecessary steps, and likely deterred consumers from completing their

    22   CCPA opt-out request. Those consumers who successfully navigated their way through these

    23   steps and submitted the webform were then directed to a "Review your Request" page where they

    24   were asked "Are you sure?" and told "Please note: Being added to our 'Do Not Sell' list may

    25   result in losing some of the customization of advertisements to your interests."

    26         13.   In addition, logged-in customers were unnecessarily burdened with this same multi-

    27   step opt-out process. When customers log into their Sling TV account, Sling TV can identify

    28   them without requiring the user to provide additional information. Nevertheless, to submit an opt-
                                                          s

 1   out request, Sling TV required these logged-in and therefore already-identified consumers to

 2   unnecessarily find and fill out a webform with their name, address, phone number, and email

 3   address-information already available to Sling TV.

 4         14.   Compounding matters, the majority of consumers access Sling TV via a Sling TV app

 5   available for various living-room devices such as TVs, players, and gaming consoles (e.g., Roku,
 6   Apple TV, Amazon Fire TV Stick, Google TV, Samsung, LG, Vizo, Tivo, Xbox). Consumers

 7   could not opt out within Sling TV's app. Instead, consumers had to use a different device with an

 8   internet connection and type the following complicated, 55-character, non-obvious URL:

 9   www.sling.com/offer-details/disclaimers/your-privacychoices. This URL directed the consumer

10   to the cookie preferences page associated with Sling TV's website. But changing cookie

11   preferences on Sling TV's website only stopped certain cookies and similar tracking technologies

12   on the specific browser used to access Sling TV's website-with no effect on Sling.TV

13   continuing to sell or share the consumer's personal information when consumers returned to

14   watching Sling TV on their living-room device via the Sling TV app. Similar to website users,

15   consumers had to realize that rejecting cookies was not the same as submitting a CCPA opt out

16   and then had to locate a link embedded in text that directed them to a separate opt-out page.

17         15.   In sum, Sling TV's opt-out methods on its website, on mobile, and on various devices

18   using the Sling TV app were not easy for consumers to execute, did not require minimal steps,

19   confused or deceived consumers, used hard-to-find links either embedded in text or unlabeled,

20   required filling out a webform - even for logged-in users, included a separate confirmation page,

21   and did not sufficiently consider the methods by which Sling TV primarily interacts with

22   consumers on app-based devices.

23        II. Sling TV Did Not Provide Sufficient Privacy Protections for Children.
24         16.   Sling TV's terms of use and other notices stated that its service is not intended to be

25   used by persons under the age of 18. But Sling TV knew that children under 16 years old watch

26   Sling TV. Sling TV includes channels and programming directed to children. Programmers notify

27   Sling TV when their programming licensed to Sling TV is directed to children. Sling TV also

28   offers consumers the ability to use parental controls. Sling TV has access to demographic,
                                                      6
                                                COMPLAINT
 1   interest, and other account-level data about its consumers and their households obtained from data

 2   brokers and other third-party vendors, including the presence of children under 16 years old in the

 3   household. Further, Sling TV uses data about the presence of children in the household, and, in

 4   some cases, that the children are within certain age ranges, to build specific groups of viewers

 5   that can be targeted for cross-context behavioral advertising.

 6         17.   Sling TV implemented advertising restrictions for certain channels targeting

 7   audiences under 13 years of age. However, Sling TV did not implement sufficient privacy

 8   protections for children. Although Sling TV asked consumers "Who's Watching?" when they log-

 9   in to their account, and consumers are able to create multiple user profiles for their household,

10   Sling TV did not offer parents the ability to set one or more user profiles as a "kid's profile" that

11   turned off the collection, sale, and sharing of consumer's personal information and cross-context

12   behavioral advertising when children are watching. Nor did Sling TV age screen users or seek

13   affirmative authorization from consumers under 16 years of age, or parental "opt-in" consent for

14   children less than 13 years of age, for selling or sharing personal information when children

15   watch Sling TV.
16         18.   Sling TV did not turn off the collection, sale, and sharing of personal information or

17   cross-context behavioral advertising when parental controls were turned on or otherwise when

18   children likely were watching Sling TV.

19         19.   Sling TV did not regularly review its channels and programing to confirm channels

20   directed to children were designated for restricted advertising treatment. Some channels directed

21   to children were not appropriately designated for restricted treatment.

22         20.   Sling TV did not protect children's privacy by default and did not provide parents

23   with sufficient information and tools to protect their children's privacy.
